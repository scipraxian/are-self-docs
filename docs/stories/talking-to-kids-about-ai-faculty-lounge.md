# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Faculty Lounge

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Four of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read in the time it takes to grade a stack of papers, and they land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the teacher who has the kid for six hours a day and wonders, on a Friday afternoon at three-fifteen, what just happened.

It is built around four of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Responsibility, and Time.** And it puts a thing called SYSTEM_PROMPT(0) where you can see it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Page That Didn't Sound Like Cole

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the in-service training. Not the district memo. The real thing — the page on top of the stack at three-fifteen on a Friday that doesn't read like the kid you have been reading for seven months.

Saanvi was at her desk in Room 14 with the windows open and the late-October air coming through. The kids had been gone twenty-five minutes. The *"What I Want to Be When I Grow Up"* assignments were halfway through, marker in her hand, and she was making her way down the stack when she came to Cole's.

Cole was nine. Cole was the kid in her fourth-grade class who, at the start of the year, had written on his About-Me card under "What I'm Good At": *making my sister laugh on purpose and on accident.* Cole's voice on a page was unmistakable — short sentences, sharp jokes, the comma in the wrong place once or twice in a hopeful way.

The page in front of her opened: *In considering my future career, I have weighed several pathways and find myself drawn to the field of robotics engineering...*

Saanvi sat back.

She read the page through. It was a perfectly fine page. It was on topic. It was the right length. It used the word *moreover.* Cole did not use the word *moreover.* Cole had never used the word *moreover.* Cole would, if asked to use the word *moreover* on penalty of detention, have written *more over* and then drawn a small picture of a more standing over a less.

She put the page down. She stood up. She walked to the lounge.

---

## Chapter 2 — The Lounge

Dennis was in there. Of course Dennis was in there. Dennis was in the lounge at 3:15 on every Friday afternoon, eating an orange one section at a time over a plate so the juice didn't get on the table. He had been teaching fifth grade at this school for thirty years. He had taught the principal when the principal was eleven. He was the calmest person in the building.

"Saanvi."

"Dennis."

"You walked in like the building's on fire."

"I have a Cole problem."

"Cole as in your Cole?"

"My Cole."

"Sit down."

She sat down. She put the page on the table next to his orange plate. He picked up his reading glasses from where they had been on top of his head for the last three hours and read the page. He read the whole page, slowly, like a person who already knew exactly what was on it before he started reading.

He put the page down.

"Saanvi. Has anyone in your room talked to you about the AI."

"They haven't talked to me about the AI."

"OK."

"Has somebody talked to *you* about the AI."

"Many somebodies have talked to me about the AI. One of those somebodies is a fifth-grader named Asher, who told me — without my asking — that he uses one most evenings to write what he calls his 'first draft' of his book report and then 'edits it so it sounds like me.'"

"Dennis."

"Mm."

"What do I do with this."

Dennis took a piece of orange. He held it for a second.

"I'll tell you what I do. Then you tell me if it works for you. Deal?"

"Deal."

---

## Chapter 3 — The Asking Minute

"Every Monday morning," Dennis said, "I do ten minutes at the start of math. I call it the Asking Minute, but it's ten minutes. I write a question on the whiteboard. The question is: *what did you ask an AI this weekend.* And then we sit. And the kids talk. And I listen. I don't grade it. I don't write it down. I don't tell their parents. *They* tell me."

"And."

"And what they tell me is mostly lovely. They asked it to make a joke about their dog. They asked it whether their idea for a book was any good. They asked it to explain the parts of a cell again because they didn't get it the first time and they were too embarrassed to ask *me.* They asked it what other kids in other countries eat for breakfast. Asher asked it to write his book report for him. Asher told the whole class he asked it to write his book report for him. And then we talked about what asking a thing to do your work *for* you is, and what asking a thing to think *with* you is, and the difference between the two."

"Did Asher get in trouble."

"Asher got asked one good question by his teacher in front of his class. His teacher asked him: *who is doing the learning when the AI writes your report?* And Asher said, very quietly, *the AI is.* And then we talked about that. He hasn't given me an AI-written book report since. He has, three times, told me at the start of his report that he 'asked the AI for help thinking about it.' Which is what I asked him to do. So that, I treat as honesty. Which I reward."

"Dennis."

"Mm."

"I'm not in fifth grade. I'm in fourth. Cole is nine."

"I know. The Asking Minute works at nine. It works *better* at nine. They haven't built the wall yet at nine."

"What about the page."

"The page is your invitation. You sit with Cole on Monday. Not in front of the class — just at his desk. You hand him the page. You say: *Cole, this doesn't sound like you. I want to know what happened.* You say it without your shoulders. You say it like you're curious. Because you are."

"What if he lies."

"He'll lie a little. They all lie a little at first. Then he'll tell you. Because Cole is the kid who wrote on his About-Me card that he likes making his sister laugh on accident. Cole is not a kid who can stand it when the room is uncomfortable. He'll tell you."

Saanvi looked at the page.

"And then what."

"Then you ask him a different question. You ask: *what did you ask the AI, before it gave you this?* Because here is the other thing. There is a long letter the company writes to the AI *before* the AI ever talks to your students. The letter tells the AI what voice to use, what to refuse, who to be, what to avoid. Cole didn't get that letter. You didn't get that letter. The AI got it. They call it a system prompt. I call it the gate."

"Dennis."

"Mm."

"How long have you been thinking about this."

"About a year and a half, dear. It snuck up on me. I stopped grading writing in my classroom a year ago when I realized I was grading the AIs and not the kids. I've been doing the Asking Minute since January."

"And the district."

"The district sent us a memo in March that told us to 'remain vigilant.' That is what the district sent us."

He took another piece of orange.

---

## Chapter 4 — What Doesn't Come Back

Saanvi sat with that.

"Dennis. There's the other piece. The one about the letter. The one about what the kids type in."

"Mm."

"Cole typed something into that thing. Whatever Cole typed went somewhere."

"It went to a server. The company keeps it. They use it to make the next version of the AI a little smarter. They use a lot of conversations. A lot of fourth-graders' conversations."

"Cole."

"Cole. Asher. Maya. Ben. Jada. The whole second period. The whole third period. The whole school."

"Dennis. They're nine."

"They're nine. And what they typed — what they were curious about, what they were afraid of, what they thought was a good joke that fell flat — none of it comes back. There is no shelf you can take it back from. We are part of the first generation of teachers whose students grew up giving their first uncertain questions to a machine that kept them. We don't have a playbook for that yet."

"What do we do."

"We *make* a playbook. We do it kid by kid. We start by talking. We start by *not* punishing the kid who tells the truth, because the kid who tells the truth is doing the harder thing and we want more of it, not less. We start by being the adult who knows what a system prompt is when the kid doesn't, the way we are the adult who knows what a thesaurus is when the kid doesn't. We start by sitting on Monday at Cole's desk. We start where we can start."

He folded the orange peel into the napkin.

"Saanvi. Please don't take this on as something you have to fix in a weekend. You have eight years in this room. You will be doing this work for the rest of your career. The school will be doing it for the rest of the school's existence. Sit at Cole's desk on Monday. That is enough for Friday."

Saanvi looked at the page on the table, and at Dennis, and out the window of the lounge at the picnic tables in the courtyard where the after-school program had a kid with a basketball walking back and forth past the fence.

"Thanks, Dennis."

"Go home. Eat something. The page will be here Monday."

---

## Chapter 5 — Monday Morning

Monday. Eight forty-seven AM. The kids were settling in. Cole was at his desk, putting his backpack under the chair.

Saanvi knelt next to him. She put the page on his desk.

"Cole."

"Hi, Ms. Patel."

"This doesn't sound like you. I want to know what happened."

She said it without her shoulders.

Cole looked at the page. He looked at her. He looked at the page again.

"…I asked the chatbot."

"You did."

"My mom said I could use it for homework if I wanted help. I asked it to write the assignment for me. Then I copied it down."

"Cole. Did you use the word *moreover.*"

"…no."

"Did you read the page after the chatbot wrote it."

"…not really."

"Cole. Here's the thing. I want to know what *you* think about being a robotics engineer. Or being whatever you actually want to be. Not what the chatbot thinks. Because I have been reading you for seven months and I really like the way you think. I want the page that's the way *you* think."

Cole was quiet for a second. Then, in the way kids ask the biggest questions when they are pretending not to:

"Ms. Patel. Was it OK that I asked it."

"It was OK that you asked it. It was not OK that you handed in a page that wasn't yours and told me it was. That part, you and I are going to fix. The asking part — that, we are going to talk about as a class today. Because you are not the only one who is doing it. And I want to talk about what asking a thing to think *with* you is, versus asking a thing to do your work *for* you. OK?"

"OK."

"And Cole."

"Yeah."

"What did you ask it, before it wrote the page."

"…I asked it what kids my age usually want to be when they grow up. So I could pick one."

Saanvi held very still, because something the size of the whole school was happening in her nine-year-old, and she did not want to be the thing that knocked it over.

"Cole."

"Yeah."

"What do *you* want to be when you grow up."

Cole thought for a second. Then, looking at his desk:

"I think I want to be a teacher. Like you."

Saanvi did not cry in front of the kid, because she was a professional. She had a forty-five-second window before the kid would notice. She used twenty of those seconds.

"Then write me that page. Tomorrow. In your own words. The reasons. The picture in your head. The day in the life. Deal?"

"Deal."

"Good. Get out your math book."

She stood up. She walked to the front of the room. She pulled down the whiteboard from the side wall. She wrote, in the dark-blue marker:

*WHAT DID YOU ASK AN AI THIS WEEKEND?*

She turned around. Twenty-three nine-year-olds were looking at her.

"OK kids. I want you to know something before we start. I do not have all the answers about this. Nobody does. I'm a year behind Mr. O'Connor and Mr. O'Connor is a year and a half into figuring it out himself. We are going to learn this together — me, you, your parents, the whole school. I want you to ask me what you ask the AI. I want you to teach me what you find. Deal?"

Twenty-three nine-year-olds nodded the small nod nine-year-olds nod when an adult is asking them to be partners in something.

"OK. Anybody. Hands up."

Nineteen hands went up.

She did not say *oh.* She said *oh* on the inside. On the outside she said *Maya, you first.* And Maya said *I asked it if cats can taste sweet.*

The Asking Minute had begun in Room 14.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Saanvi's pause, Dennis's orange, and Cole's eyes on his desk. They were:

- **Inquiry** — Saanvi's good move was the Monday-morning question: *Cole, this doesn't sound like you. I want to know what happened.* Inquiry is the move that makes the rest possible.
- **Humility** — Dennis has been doing this for a year and a half because he was willing to be the older teacher who didn't already know. Humility is what kept the door open long enough for the Asking Minute to walk through it.
- **Responsibility** — *We are part of the first generation of teachers whose students grew up giving their first uncertain questions to a machine that kept them.* That sentence is the Variable. Naming it as *ours,* not as somebody else's, is what makes it possible to act.
- **Time** — Dennis to Saanvi: *You will be doing this work for the rest of your career.* The Tale's stakes are the long arc. *Sit at Cole's desk on Monday* is the short one. Both are real.

The hosted AI Cole asked entered his fourth-grade brain *on* him — through the gate, on the company's terms, with a system prompt he did not see. Saanvi entered Cole's confusion *with* him. Same kid, same desk. Opposite consent geometry.

The Asking Minute is what that architecture grows into when a teacher gives twenty-three nine-year-olds permission to bring the AI into the room on the table, where it can be seen and asked about.
