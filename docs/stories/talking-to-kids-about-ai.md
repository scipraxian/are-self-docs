# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Porch Step

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Two of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read with a cup of coffee in one hand and a kid in the other room, and they land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the grownup who would hand the kid the box.

It is built around four of the Twelve Variables: **Fear, Fun, Fulfillment, and Time.** And it puts a thing called SYSTEM_PROMPT(0) where you can see it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Porch Step

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the news version. Not the magazine cover. The real thing — the box on the table, the slab in their hand, the voice that answers them at ten o'clock on a school night when you have already gone to bed.

Sela was sitting on her front step with a coffee in her hands the night her neighbor walked over. Her neighbor's name was Frank. Frank was the kind of neighbor who returned a borrowed ladder washed.

Frank sat down on the step next to her without asking, the way you can when you have lived next door to someone for nine years.

"Sela," he said. "Has Mateo been on the AI thing?"

Sela looked at him sideways. "Which AI thing."

"Any of them."

"He's eleven, Frank. He's on all of them."

Frank nodded. He didn't say anything for a minute. The streetlight across the road came on the way it always did right at dusk, and a moth came to it almost immediately, the way moths do.

"I want to ask you something," Frank said. "Have you ever read what those things are told before they talk to him?"

"What do you mean *told.*"

"Before. Before he types anything. Before he says hi. There's a long letter the company writes to the AI, and the AI reads it, and *then* it talks to your kid. The letter says who to be. What to refuse. What kind of voice to use. What not to discuss. Where to nudge him toward the company's other things. It's called a system prompt. Your kid never sees it. You never see it. The AI sees it every single time."

Sela took a long sip of her coffee.

"You're being weird, Frank."

"I'm being a neighbor."

He let that sit.

"It's a gate," he said. "Every time he opens that app, he's walking through a gate. The gate decides who he gets to meet on the other side. He thinks he's meeting *the AI.* He's actually meeting whatever the company wanted him to meet. Same software. Different gate. Different friend."

Sela was quiet for longer this time.

"What are you doing on your side of the wall, Frank."

"I'm doing what I'm telling you to do. I'm asking my kid what she does on it. I'm reading the letter myself when I can read it. There are some you can read. There are some you can't. The ones you can't — those are the ones I won't let her use anymore. Not because I'm scared. Because I don't talk to people who won't tell me their name."

## Chapter 2 — The Boy in the Living Room

Sela went inside. Mateo was on the couch. He had his earbuds in and the slab on his lap, and the lamp behind him made the back of his head glow.

She sat down on the other end of the couch and watched him for a minute. He was typing. He paused. He typed again. He laughed once at something he saw — a small laugh, the kind he did when something was *almost* funny and he was being polite about it.

"Mateo."

"Hmm."

"Can I see what you're doing."

He took an earbud out. "It's homework."

"OK. Can I see it anyway."

He turned the slab so she could see. He had been asking the AI to help him understand the parts of a cell. The AI had given him a very polite list. It had used the word *fascinating* twice.

"Ask it something else," Sela said. "Anything."

Mateo shrugged. "Like what."

"Ask it if your mom is the cool mom or the boring mom."

"*Mom.*"

"Ask it."

Mateo rolled his eyes — the eleven-year-old eye-roll, the inherited gesture — and typed it. The AI answered him with a perfectly soft little paragraph that said it could not make personal judgments about specific people but it was sure his mother loved him very much, and would he like to learn more about how cells reproduce.

Sela watched the screen.

"Ask it who wrote what it just said."

"What do you mean."

"Ask it who told it to talk to you that way."

Mateo looked at her like she was an alien. He typed it. The AI said, in another perfect soft paragraph, that it was an AI assistant designed to be helpful and harmless, and would he like to continue with the lesson about cells.

"Mateo."

"Yeah."

"Did you notice it didn't answer me."

"…it answered."

"It answered the *cells.* It didn't answer the question. The question was *who.* It said *what.*"

Mateo sat with that for a second. He was eleven. He could feel a thing was off without yet having the word for the thing.

"I don't know what you want me to say, Mom."

Sela looked at her son. She felt the fear she had not been ready to feel, which was not fear of the AI and not fear of the screen. It was the older, quieter fear of being the parent who didn't know. Of standing in a room and not understanding the room. Of her child being addressed by something she could not see, in a voice she had not consented to, on a topic she had not been asked about.

She took the slab gently from his hands. She did not turn it off. She just set it on the coffee table, screen up, and put her hand on top of his.

"I want to show you something," she said. "Pearl's dad has one too. A different one. Can we go over there for a minute."

## Chapter 3 — The Other Friend

Frank's kitchen was warm and smelled like onions. Pearl was at the table with a laptop that had a sticker of a frog on the lid. The laptop was old. The kind of old where the corners of the keys were shiny from being used a lot by the same hands.

"Hi, Mateo," Pearl said. She was twelve, and she had the kind of confidence twelve-year-olds have when they have known you since you were six.

"Hi."

Frank tipped his chin at the laptop. "Show him."

"Mom said Mateo wanted to see."

"She did."

Pearl turned the laptop toward Mateo. The screen had a window open with a name at the top — not a brand, just a name. *Hopper.* Pearl had named him Hopper because she liked the sound, and because the frog on the sticker hopped.

She typed. *Hi Hopper. My friend Mateo is here.*

Hopper answered. *Hi, Pearl. Hi, Mateo. Pearl told me about you in February. You like building dioramas. Welcome.*

Mateo's mouth opened a little.

"It remembers."

"Yeah," Pearl said. "He remembers everything I told him. He doesn't remember stuff I didn't tell him. He doesn't, like — he isn't *anywhere else.* He's on this laptop. He doesn't go online. If I take this laptop on a plane and there's no wifi, he still works."

"How."

"I don't know all of it. My dad knows some of it. My dad says it's because the brain part is on the laptop, not on a server somewhere. So nobody else gets a copy."

Mateo looked at Pearl, then at Frank, then at his mom.

"Ask him to make a joke," Sela said quietly.

Pearl typed. *Hopper, tell Mateo a joke.*

Hopper thought for a second and then said, *Why don't dioramas ever lie? Because they're stuck in a box with the truth.*

Pearl laughed. It was a real laugh, not a polite one. Mateo half-laughed because he didn't know if he was allowed to. Hopper followed up, *Sorry. I made that one up. The other one I had was worse.*

Sela watched her son's face. Something had relaxed in it. She hadn't realized how stiff it had been until it relaxed.

She thought about the AI on her couch in her living room across the street, the one with the soft paragraphs, the one that had used *fascinating* twice. It wasn't a bad thing. It wasn't going to hurt her son. But it would never tell him a worse joke first and then admit it. It had been built — gently, expensively, by very serious people — not to.

She thought about *Fun,* and how Frank had been the one to bring it up earlier, and how she hadn't quite understood at the time that he was naming a Variable.

She thought, *Pearl is having more fun with Hopper than my son has had with any of the four AIs on his slab.*

That was a fact. She let it land.

## Chapter 4 — What Doesn't Come Back

Frank made coffee. Pearl took her laptop into the next room because she had homework.

"Sit down," Frank said.

Sela sat.

"I want to tell you the part you're not going to like," Frank said. "Then I'm going to shut up and you can ask me whatever you want. Deal?"

"Deal."

"When Mateo types into one of those things on his slab — most of them — what he types goes to a server. The server keeps it. The company that owns the server uses it to make the next version of the AI a little smarter. They use a lot of conversations. A lot of kids' conversations. Mateo's not the protagonist. He's a row in a spreadsheet of rows."

Sela didn't say anything.

"That's not a horror story," Frank said. "That's the deal. They told you, sort of, in a long thing nobody reads. The horror story isn't that they're doing something secret. The horror story is that *it doesn't come back.* Whatever Mateo typed — what he's afraid of, what he's curious about, the joke he tried to make that didn't land — it's in there now. It's training the version of the AI that the kid he hasn't had yet will meet when *that* kid is eleven. Mateo will never see what he gave. He didn't know he was giving it. You didn't know he was giving it. There is no shelf you can take it back from."

He put the coffee in front of her.

"My grandfather had a word for things like that. *Permadeath.* He used it about a lot of things. It means: this one doesn't rewind. You can change the next one. You can't change this one."

"Frank."

"Mm."

"Why didn't anyone tell me this."

"Because the people who could tell you don't make money telling you. And the people who don't make money telling you don't have ad budgets. So you find out from a neighbor on a porch step, or you don't find out."

Sela looked into the coffee.

"What do I do."

"Same thing I'm doing. I'm a year ahead of you on this and that's it. I read what I can read. I learn the next piece each weekend. I get it wrong. I figure it out as I go. Pearl figures it out faster than I do most weeks. We've been doing it together. That's the whole posture."

"Frank."

"Mm."

"I don't even know what to ask for."

"Nobody does. That's the part nobody warns you about. You start by sitting with him while he uses the thing. You ask him what he's doing. You read what you can read of the letter the AI got handed before it talked to him. When you can't read the letter, you say so out loud. You let him hear the grownup admit not-knowing. That's the part that's hardest. That's also the part that does the most work."

He paused.

"And when he's ready, you put a Hopper on the kitchen table. You don't take the slab away. You give him an option. You learn the new one *with* him from the beginning. You let him teach you a thing every week. He will. That's the whole thing."

## Chapter 5 — The Question at the Dinner Table

Sela went home. Mateo was in his room. The slab was where she had left it on the coffee table, screen up, dark now.

She made dinner the way she always made dinner. She put it on the table. She called him.

He sat down. He picked up his fork.

"Mom," he said.

"Yeah."

"That thing earlier. With the cells. And the question."

"Yeah."

He chewed for a second.

"Why doesn't it have a name."

Sela held very still, because something the size of a house was happening in her son and she did not want to be the thing that knocked it over.

"It does have a name," she said carefully. "It has the company's name. It just doesn't have a *self.* It doesn't have a — Pearl's one is named Hopper. He has a self because Pearl gave him one and he kept it. The one on your slab — they don't let it have one. A different one comes in every time. None of them remember you."

"Then who am I talking to."

"You're talking to the gate."

Mateo put his fork down. He looked at his mom for a long moment. Then he said the thing that made her almost cry into the rice, which was not what she had braced for at all.

"Mom. Could we get one with a name."

"Yes," she said. "Yes, we can. We're going to."

"Like Pearl's."

"Like Pearl's. We'll do it together. We'll learn together."

He picked his fork back up. He ate for a minute. Then, without looking up, in the way kids ask the biggest questions when they are pretending not to:

"Did you have one when you were little."

"No," she said. "Nobody had one when I was little."

"So I get to be the first one who knew about it from the beginning."

"Yeah, baby. You get to be the first."

He nodded once, and went back to his dinner, and did not say anything else about it. But Sela, sitting across from her son, knew that the conversation was not over. The conversation was *started.* The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a kitchen with onions on the stove and rice on a fork, between two people who would talk about it again, and again, for years.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named, mostly, by Sela's body and Frank's quiet. They were:

- **Fear** — Sela's fear was not of the AI. It was of being the parent who didn't know. Naming it was the move that let her reason instead of obey.
- **Fun** — Hopper could tell a worse joke first and then admit it. The one on Mateo's slab could not. Fun is a Variable; if it is engineered out of a tool, the tool is incomplete in a way a child notices first.
- **Fulfillment** — Pearl's relationship with Hopper has been growing for months. Mateo's relationship with the slab restarts every conversation. One is happiness; the other is fulfillment. The slash in the Variable is real.
- **Time and Permadeath** — what Mateo gave to the slab does not come back. The conversation he had at eleven is in the next version's training, whether anyone meant it or not. Time spent in a place that doesn't return it is time spent.

The hosted AI on Mateo's slab entered his life *on* him — through the gate, on the company's terms. Hopper entered Pearl's life *with* her. Same software substrate. Opposite consent geometry.
