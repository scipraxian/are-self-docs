# Have You Talked to Your Kids About Social Media? — A School Board Meeting

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Six of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read between the consent agenda and the public comment period, and they land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the grownup with the vote in front of her — the school board member, the council member, the legislator, the person whose hand goes up *aye* or *nay* in a room of strangers and changes some part of how four thousand kids spend their day.

It is built around five of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Responsibility, Time, and Permadeath.** And it puts a thing called *the agenda packet* where you can see it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Briefing Packet

Have you talked to your kids about social media?

Not the editorial. Not the cable-news segment. The real thing — the eighty-page packet on your kitchen table on a Sunday night that you have to read before Tuesday because four thousand kids in your district will live differently on Wednesday morning depending on what you do.

Inez was at her kitchen table in her reading glasses with a yellow legal pad and a cup of decaf and the packet open to page forty-three. The cat — Bruno, sixteen years old, indifferent to policy — was on the chair next to hers. The legal pad had a column of *yes* questions and a column of *more questions* and the *more* column was longer.

The agenda item was Resolution 2026-14: *Personal Communications Device Policy — District-Wide Implementation.* The plain-English version was: phones in lockers all day, not in pockets. The more-than-plain-English version was eighty pages of staff report, exemption framework, transition plan, equity review, two minority reports from the Student Advisory Council, and a public-comment summary with seventy-two written submissions, of which Inez had read twenty-eight.

Inez had been a school board member for seven years. Before that she had been a pediatric nurse for twenty-eight, mostly nights. She knew what it was to spend an entire shift with kids whose parents weren't there. She knew what it was to make a decision affecting a child she would not see again. She knew what a long arc looked like.

She did not, until this packet, know what *Snap streak* meant. She had asked her grandson Lucas about it on Sunday at lunch. Lucas was twelve. Lucas had explained Snap streaks for nine minutes without taking a breath. Inez had taken notes.

She wrote, in her *more questions* column: *what does the policy do for kids whose home life depends on the phone being in their pocket — emergency contact, parent-shift-change pickup, custody-handoff text from a non-custodial parent.* She underlined the line.

She wrote, underneath: *who in this packet has actually talked to a kid.*

She put the pencil down. She drank the decaf, which had gotten cold. She thought about Lucas at the table on Sunday, mid-Snap-streak-explanation, his hands moving the way her late husband's used to when he was telling a story. Lucas was a good boy. Lucas had a phone because Sandra worked late shifts and the phone was the bridge.

She wrote, in the *yes* column: *the policy is good in shape.*

She wrote, in the *more* column: *I want to hear from a kid on Tuesday before I vote.*

She closed the packet. She made another cup of decaf. She started page forty-four.

## Chapter 2 — The Meeting Begins

Tuesday. Seven PM. The board chamber smelled like the carpet cleaner the district used after the weekend tournaments, and like the coffee the district provided in the back at half-strength. Inez sat at her usual seat at the dais, fourth from the left, between the superintendent's empty chair and Walt Yoon's full one.

Walt was sixty. Walt had taught high-school English at Lincoln for thirty years before he ran for the board. Walt's reading glasses lived on top of his head except when he needed them, in which case they came down his face one inch at a time, like he was lowering a drawbridge. Walt was the calmest person on the dais. He had taught two of the board's other members when they were sixteen.

"Inez."

"Walt."

"You read the whole packet."

"I read the whole packet."

"You're the only one."

"I'm not the only one."

"Sandra's the only other one."

"Sandra reads everything. Sandra is a school librarian. Sandra is paid to read everything."

"Sandra is paid to read books to seven-year-olds. Sandra reads the packet because Sandra is conscientious."

"Walt."

"What."

"I have questions."

"Of course you have questions. You're you."

"I want to hear from a kid before I vote."

Walt looked at her over the top of his reading glasses, which had come halfway down his face.

"Inez. There are nine kids signed up for public comment. The chair is going to give them the first slot before the adults so they don't have to sit through the rest of us. I asked. Marlene moved them up."

"Walt. Bless you."

"I didn't do anything. Marlene did it. Sit. The chair is gaveling in."

The chair gaveled in. The pledge happened. The consent agenda passed in seven seconds. The chair looked up.

"Public comment on Resolution 2026-14. We have seventeen sign-ups, nine of whom are students. Per board practice we are going to take the student speakers first. Three minutes each. Adaeze Okonkwo, you are first. Please come to the microphone."

A girl stood up from the third row. Inez had not noticed her. Inez noticed her now. Adaeze was thirteen, tall for thirteen, in a jean jacket and a pair of high-tops. She walked to the microphone. She put her index card on the lectern. She did not look up from it.

The room got quiet.

---

## Chapter 3 — The Kid Who Spoke

"Hi," Adaeze said into the microphone. "My name is Adaeze Okonkwo. I am in eighth grade at Roosevelt Middle School. I am here to ask you a question. I am also here to ask you to think harder than the resolution is asking you to think."

She paused. She did not look up.

"When I was eleven I posted a video. It was supposed to be funny. For two weeks it made me popular. For two weeks the count went up. Then for two weeks the count went up the other way. Then I switched schools. The video is still up. I cannot make it not be up. Other accounts I do not know put it on their accounts. The video is going to be up when I am twenty-five.

"I would not have made the video that day if my phone had been in my locker. That is true. So part of me wants to ask you to vote yes.

"But the phone in my locker doesn't help me when I get home and the phone is in my pocket again. The phone in my locker doesn't teach me what I needed to know that I didn't know that day at eleven. What I needed was somebody — an adult, somewhere — to have shown me what a video like that does, and how the algorithm picks it up, and what a view count actually is, and how the count changes when the wind changes. I needed somebody to have learned this *with* me before I posted, not to have taken the phone away after.

"So this is my question. Please figure out how to teach us what we are doing before we do it. Don't just lock it away from us during lunch. *Teach* us. Use my video if you need to. I am thirteen now. I would tell the eleven-year-olds what I wish someone had told me, if you put me in the room. I would help.

"Thank you for letting me speak. That is all I have."

She picked up her index card. She walked back to the third row. She sat down between two adults who Inez assumed were her parents and who Inez would later learn were her aunt and her aunt's wife.

The chair did not call the next student for three full seconds. Three seconds is a long time at a public meeting.

Inez looked at Walt. Walt was looking at his hands, which were folded on the dais in front of him, perfectly still.

The chair called the next student. The next student was nine. The next student talked about how he was scared to put the phone in his locker because his mom said to keep it on him in case of an earthquake, and could the policy please have an earthquake exemption, because he had a list of what was in his earthquake-emergency-pocket and the phone was on the list. The room laughed, gently, in the way rooms laugh at a nine-year-old who has thought hard about earthquakes. The nine-year-old did not laugh. He went back to his seat. The chair called the next student.

Inez wrote down, on her legal pad, *Adaeze.* She wrote down, underneath: *the video is still up.* She underlined that line three times.

She wrote down, in her *more questions* column: *what does the district do for kids who have already made the video.*

She did not have an answer to that question. The packet did not have an answer either. Inez, in her chair at the dais, with eight more student speakers still to come, knew that question was hers now whether she had asked for it or not.

---

## Chapter 4 — At the Break

The chair called a five-minute break before the staff presentation. Inez and Walt walked out into the hallway together. The hallway had the institutional fluorescent that turned every face the same color, which Inez had stopped noticing fifteen years ago.

"Walt."

"Mm."

"That kid just rerouted my night."

"Mine too."

"I came in here to vote yes. Adaeze just told me — politely, plainly, in front of seventeen sign-ups and a chair with a clipboard — that voting yes by itself is the lazy answer. That is what she just told us. *Teach us. Don't just lock it away.*"

"That is what she said."

"Walt. I do not think I know enough to vote yes tonight."

"OK."

"I have been on this board for seven years. I read the whole packet. I am the conscientious one. And I have a thirteen-year-old in the third row telling me that the packet is asking the wrong question. She is right. The packet is asking the wrong question."

"What do you want to do."

"I want to move to table the resolution for ninety days. I want to convene a working group with at least three students on it — Adaeze first, by name, if she'll do it — and I want the working group to come back with a packaged proposal. Whatever the right phone policy is, plus the curriculum that goes with it, plus the co-design framework that puts kids on the page where the rules get drafted. *Then* we vote. Not before."

"Inez."

"Mm."

"You know the motion to table will probably fail. The room is fed up. The parents in the audience are fed up. Most of the board is fed up. The fed-up vote is yes. The fed-up vote is the easy vote."

"I know."

"And if it fails, the resolution will be in front of you for an up-or-down."

"I know."

"How are you voting on the up-or-down."

"No. I am voting no. With a statement on the record. *I do not know enough yet to make this call.* Because I don't. And the kid who just spoke knows more about it than I do, and her question is the better one."

Walt looked at her over the top of his reading glasses, which had come the rest of the way down his face.

"OK Inez. I will second your motion to table. I will probably also vote no on the resolution if the table fails. I have been thinking about this for a year and a half and tonight is the first time I have heard a person under fifteen ask the right question out loud in this chamber. That counts for something."

"Walt. Bless you again."

"Don't bless me. I should have said this six months ago without a kid having to walk to a microphone first."

The chair came around the corner of the hallway with her clipboard.

"Two minutes," she said.

Inez and Walt walked back into the chamber.

---

## Chapter 5 — The Vote, Then Home

The staff presentation took thirty-eight minutes. The adult public comment took fifty-one. The board discussion took twenty-three.

At the end of the discussion, before the chair could call the vote, Inez raised her hand.

"Madame Chair. I move to table Resolution 2026-14 for ninety days, and to direct the superintendent to convene a working group of staff, parents, and at least three students — Adaeze Okonkwo first, by name, if she will accept — to bring back a packaged proposal that combines whatever phone policy the working group recommends with the curriculum and the co-design framework Ms. Okonkwo asked us to consider. *Teach us. Don't just lock it away.* Those are her words. I want them on the record."

"Second," Walt said.

The chair called the vote on the motion to table.

It failed two to five.

The chair called the vote on Resolution 2026-14.

Inez asked to speak before the vote. The chair gave her thirty seconds.

"I will be voting no. I want the record to show that I am not voting no because I oppose action. I am voting no because I do not know enough yet to make this call, and the thirteen-year-old who spoke at this microphone two hours ago knows more about it than I do, and her question is the better one. I will spend the next ninety days doing the work she asked us to do, with the working group I just tried to fund. I hope this room joins me on Wednesday morning to start that work. Thank you."

The chair called the roll.

Resolution 2026-14 passed five to two. Inez and Walt voted no.

Under New Business, Inez moved that the board direct the superintendent to convene the working group anyway — staff, parents, and at least three students — to draft a companion resolution within ninety days addressing both the lifecycle of student social-media content already in circulation *and* the curriculum that should run alongside the phone policy that just passed. *The video is still up* policy AND the *teach-us* policy. Walt seconded. The motion carried six to one. The one *no* was on the grounds of needing more information, which was what Inez had voted *no* on twice in her seven years and which she did not begrudge.

The chair adjourned at ten-fourteen.

Inez packed up her legal pad. She walked out into the parking lot. The night was cool. She drove home with the radio off.

She got home at ten-forty-six. Bruno met her at the door. She fed Bruno. She poured herself a glass of water.

She picked up the phone. She called Sandra.

"Mom."

"Hi honey. Did the meeting go OK."

"The resolution passed. Five to two. Walt and I voted no. The motion to table failed first. The working-group motion under New Business passed six to one."

"You voted no?"

"I voted no. There was a thirteen-year-old at the microphone tonight who told us we were asking the wrong question. She was right. So I voted no and stood up the working group anyway. Adaeze Okonkwo. I'm going to call her family in the morning."

"OK."

"Sandra."

"Yeah."

"How's Lucas."

"He's asleep. He has science early."

"Sandra. Has Lucas put anything on the internet that he can't take back."

A pause. Not a long one. The pause of a parent who has thought about it before.

"…Probably. He's twelve, Mom. Probably yes. I don't know what."

"Have you and Lucas looked together."

"No. Not really. Not the whole thing."

"Honey. Will you do me a favor and look this weekend. Just look. Sit on the couch with him. Don't make him feel like he did something wrong. Just look."

"…OK."

"Sandra."

"Yeah."

"There was a thirteen-year-old at the meeting tonight. Adaeze. She walked up to the microphone and told seven board members that her video from when she was eleven is still up. Her own video. About her own self. She cannot take it down because other accounts have it. She switched schools because of it. And then she told us not to just lock the phones away — to *teach* her, and the kids coming up behind her, what we are doing before we do it. She offered to be the one in the room helping. She is somebody's grandkid, Sandra. I want to be the grandma who learned with."

A longer pause this time.

"Mom. OK. I'll look on Saturday."

"Tell me what you find."

"OK."

"And Sandra."

"Mm."

"If you want me to look with you. If two of us looking is easier."

"…I might want that, Mom. I might."

"OK."

They hung up. Inez sat at the kitchen table. Bruno got into her lap, which Bruno was too old to do gracefully and which Inez did not stop him from doing. She thought about Lucas, asleep in his bed across town, and Adaeze, asleep in her bed somewhere in the district, and the four thousand kids who would put their phones in their lockers on Wednesday morning, and the videos that were still up that the policy did not touch.

She wrote, on a fresh page in the legal pad, the heading: *the working group — draft.*

Underneath, she wrote: *who is on the working group.* And then, on the second line: *at least three students. Adaeze first. Then two younger.*

On the third line: *what we don't know yet.* That column was longer than the first.

She turned the page. She drank the water. She went to bed at eleven-thirty.

In the morning, Wednesday, four thousand kids put their phones in their lockers. Some of them grumbled. Some of them, the ones who had been waiting for the building to give them permission to put the phone down, were quietly relieved. Most of them were neither. Most of them were just going to school.

The video was still up.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a kitchen at eleven PM with a sixteen-year-old cat in a grandmother's lap, between an elected official and her daughter and a grandson asleep across town, who would talk about this again, and again, for years.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Inez's pencil and Walt's reading glasses and Adaeze's three seconds of silence at the microphone. They were:

- **Inquiry** — Inez's *who in this packet has actually talked to a kid* on Sunday night was the move that made the rest of the Tale possible. The vote-no statement at the dais — *I do not know enough yet to make this call, and the thirteen-year-old who spoke at this microphone two hours ago knows more about it than I do, and her question is the better one* — was the same Variable, on the record. Inquiry that costs you a vote in your own caucus is the real version of Inquiry.
- **Humility** — Inez had been on the board for seven years and did not know what *Snap streak* meant before Sunday lunch with Lucas. She wrote it down. By Tuesday night she was willing to say *I do not know enough yet* into a live microphone in front of the parents who elected her. Humility is what made the reroute possible.
- **Inclusion** — Adaeze got three minutes at a microphone, and then — when Inez stood the working group up — she got an actual seat. The seat is the Variable. Testimony plus seat is Inclusion. Testimony alone is theater.
- **Responsibility** — Walt: *I should have said this six months ago without a kid having to walk to a microphone first.* Naming the failure as your own, before anyone else names it for you, is the Variable. Inez and Walt named theirs together in the hallway and acted on it inside fifteen minutes.
- **Time and Permadeath** — *The video is still up. I cannot make it not be up.* Adaeze's plain sentence is still the cleanest naming of Variable Twelve in any of the Tales. The phone policy that passed prevented some next-Adaezes. It did not undo Adaeze. The undo problem and the *what we should have been teaching* problem both belong to the working group that started in Inez's kitchen at eleven PM with Bruno in her lap.

The platform that Adaeze posted to entered her eleven-year-old life *on* her — through the algorithm, on the company's terms, with consequences that did not unwind. The phone policy that passed entered four thousand kids' lives *on* them too — a policy made by adults about kids' days, voted in over the dissent of the two board members who had been listening hardest to a kid. The working group Inez is starting on Wednesday morning is the *with* version: Adaeze in the room, two younger students with her, the curriculum and the co-design framework drafted alongside the phone policy rather than instead of the kids. Same district. Different consent geometry. The shape is choosable. Inez chose. Five others chose differently. The work begins anyway, because Inez put in the New Business motion to fund it whether the resolution passed or didn't.

### If you want to know what other people are doing in school-board rooms right now

The phones-in-schools fight is one of the most active K–12 policy fronts in the United States. A short map of who is doing what:

- **The Phone Free Schools Movement** (founded 2023) is the most visible advocacy organization for *phones in lockers all day* policies. Their playbook is close to the one Inez voted for in this Tale.
- **Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024)** is the book that landed under most of these board members' Sunday-night packets. Its argument is that smartphone-and-social-media adoption between 2010 and 2015 caused a measurable adolescent mental-health crisis, and that schools removing phones is part of the response.
- **State-level legislation:** as of 2025, several US states have passed or are debating statewide K–12 phone-policy mandates — California's AB 3216 (the Phone-Free Schools Act, signed September 2024) and Florida's HB 379 (2023) are the most-cited examples. Your district's vote is often downstream of these.
- **Voices on the other side** of the policy fight worth reading include critics who point out that bans relocate use rather than reduce harm, and that working-class kids — whose parents may rely on phone contact during the school day — are disproportionately affected. **Common Sense Media,** the **Center for Humane Technology,** and the **National Association for Media Literacy Education (NAMLE)** publish curriculum-plus-co-design materials that are slower-burn than the bans but aim further upstream.
- **For the *what do we do about the videos that are already up* problem** — the working-group thread Inez opened under New Business at the end of this Tale — the shape of an answer is still being drafted. **Internet Safety Labs** (founded 2022) is one organization working on it. The discourse is much less mature than the ban discourse. Worth watching.

These voices don't agree with each other. They don't all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, hold the disagreements open, and decide with the kids actually in the room.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.
