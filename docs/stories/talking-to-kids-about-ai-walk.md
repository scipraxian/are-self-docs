# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Walk

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Eleven of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

This is the grandmother's Tale. The grownup who is the only grownup in the house — because the parents are deployed, or working three jobs, or just gone — and who is figuring out a thing she did not sign up to figure out, with the kid who needs her to figure it out anyway.

It is built around five of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Inclusion, Responsibility, and Perseverance.** And it puts a thing called *the AI-generated image* on the kitchen table where it can be looked at and not flinched from.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Quiet Week

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the news version. Not the church-mom version. The real thing — the twelve-year-old in the bedroom across the hall who has been quieter this week than he was last week, and quieter last week than the week before, and you are seventy-two years old and you do not know what is wrong but you know that something is.

Ruth was at the kitchen table at eight-forty-five on a Saturday morning in October with the crossword and her coffee. The leaves on the maple in the front yard had turned the deep red they turned every year about now. Ruth had lived in this house for forty-one years. She had buried Edward five years ago in the cemetery on Lawson Road. She had started raising Marcus three years ago when Caroline and Aaron had been deployed the second time, and the second time had become three years, and three years had become *we'll let you know.*

Caroline was Ruth's daughter. Caroline was a Captain. Aaron was Ruth's son-in-law. Aaron was a Major. They were both somewhere Ruth was not allowed to know the name of, and they sent letters once a month, and they video-called when the satellite cooperated, and Marcus was twelve.

Marcus had been quiet for a week.

Not the angry kind of quiet. Not the I-don't-want-to-talk-about-it kind. The smaller kind — the kind where the kid still sat at the table for dinner, still answered when she asked, still said *good night Gramma* on his way to bed, but where the *something* underneath had gotten smaller. Less of him at the table. Less of him in the room.

Ruth had been a teacher's aide for thirty-one years before she retired. Ruth knew this kind of quiet. Ruth had seen it in a hundred kids over a hundred Octobers. Ruth knew it was not a *leave him alone* kind of quiet. It was a *somebody-please-notice* kind of quiet that the kid had decided would have to be noticed without being asked-about because the kid did not have words for it yet.

Ruth had been waiting all week for the right moment to knock.

The right moment was now. Saturday. No school. No homework hovering. The whole day in front of them. She closed the crossword. She stood up. She walked across the hall.

She knocked on Marcus's door.

"Marcus."

"…Yeah Gramma."

"Can I come in for a minute."

A pause. The pause was longer than it should have been. Ruth waited.

"…OK."

## Chapter 2 — The Image

Marcus was on his bed in his Saturday clothes, which were the same clothes he wore most days, which were jeans and a sweatshirt that had been Aaron's and that Ruth had washed too many times and that still smelled faintly like the wood-stain Aaron had used in the garage in 2020. The phone was face down next to him on the bedspread. The bedspread was the one Edward had picked out at the JCPenney in 1997.

Ruth sat down on the edge of the bed. She did not touch the phone. She did not ask about the phone.

"Marcus. You've been quiet this week. I want to ask you what's going on. You don't have to tell me. I want you to know I'm asking."

Marcus did not look at her. Marcus looked at the phone.

"…There's a thing on my phone. A kid at school sent it to me. I haven't shown anyone."

"OK."

"It's a picture. Of me. Doing a thing I didn't do."

Ruth held very still.

"Marcus. Will you tell me more about it."

"It's an AI picture. A kid in my grade made it. He used one of those apps. He put my face on it. It's me — in the picture I'm — I'm in my underwear at a basketball game. I never went to a basketball game in my underwear, Gramma. The picture is fake. But it looks real. He sent it to four other kids on Monday. By Wednesday eight kids had seen it. By Thursday I think maybe twenty. I don't know. Today is Saturday and I have been waiting for you to be busy enough not to ask."

Ruth did not say anything for a long minute. She did not say *oh Marcus.* She did not say *I'm so sorry.* She did not say *show me.* She just sat with him, on the edge of his bed, in Aaron's old sweatshirt, with the phone face down between them.

She took a breath.

"Marcus. Honey. Thank you for telling me. I want to ask you for one thing first before we figure out what to do. Will you let me see the picture? You don't have to. I am asking."

Marcus picked up the phone. He turned it over. He did the thing on the screen. He handed it to her.

Ruth looked at it. The picture was, the way Marcus had said, fake. The face was Marcus's. The body was somebody else's. The setting was a basketball game Marcus had not attended. The other kids in the photo were laughing at someone. The someone was in the middle. The someone had Marcus's face on it.

Ruth had not looked at an AI-generated image before. She had heard about them on the news. She had not understood, until she was looking at one of her grandson's face on a body that was not his, what they were going to mean for kids his age.

She handed the phone back to him, screen down.

"Marcus. The picture is fake. The picture is wrong. The kid who made it did a wrong thing. Not a small wrong thing. A big wrong thing. I want you to know I see that, before we do anything else. The wrong is on him, not on you. You hear me?"

"…yeah Gramma."

"Marcus. We're going to take a walk. I need to think out loud and I do better walking. We're going to walk to Fred's and get a Snickers and we're going to figure out what to do. Put on a coat. The leaves are good today. Come on."

---

## Chapter 3 — The Walk

Fred's was the corner store at the end of Elmwood, six blocks down. The walk was mostly downhill on the way there. The maple leaves were on the sidewalk. They scuffed under their feet the way leaves did. Marcus was in his coat with his hands in his pockets and his head down and the phone in his back pocket, screen facing in.

Ruth talked while they walked. She talked the way she had talked to first-graders for thirty-one years — slowly, in plain language, without dressing the hard parts up.

"Marcus. I am seventy-two years old. I do not understand AI. I have not used it. I do not know how the apps work. I do not know how the kid at school made the picture. I am going to learn. I am telling you this so you know I know I do not know yet. OK?"

"…OK Gramma."

"I am going to call Mrs. Holzer at the school on Monday morning. Not because I want to get the boy in trouble — well, I do want him in trouble, and I am being honest with you about that. But the reason I am calling Mrs. Holzer is to find out what the school is doing about this kind of thing. Because if it's happening to you, it is happening to other kids in your grade. The school needs to know. Mrs. Holzer is going to want to know. The boy who did this needs an adult to sit with him and explain what he just did. That is not your job. That is the adults' job. I am going to be one of those adults."

"…OK Gramma."

"Marcus. Honey. The picture is going to be there for a while. I am not going to lie to you about that. The kids who saw it are going to remember it. Some of them are going to remember it for a long time. We can't undo it. What we can do — what *I* can do, with you and not at you — is make sure that the boy who made it gets stopped from making the next one. And make sure that the school understands what the new shape of bullying looks like with these tools. And make sure *you* know that what those kids saw is not who you are. They saw a fake. The fake doesn't get to be you."

Marcus walked for half a block without saying anything. Then:

"Gramma. I was scared you would say I shouldn't have a phone."

Ruth stopped walking. She turned to look at him.

"Marcus. The phone is not the problem. The phone is how you and I and your mom talk on Sundays. The phone is how you call me when you need me. Taking the phone away would be the answer your mom would give if your mom didn't know what else to do. *I am going to figure out what else to do.* I might decide we change something about how the phone works. I will not take it away as a punishment to you for somebody else doing a wrong thing. That would be backwards and you know it would be backwards."

"…OK Gramma."

"And one more thing. I am going to learn what the AI apps are. I am going to learn this weekend. I am going to ask you to help me learn, because you know the apps better than I do, and you are twelve and I am seventy-two and the only way I am going to catch up is if you teach me what you know and I figure out the rest. Are you OK with that?"

"…yeah Gramma."

"Good. I want a Snickers. You want a Snickers?"

"…can I have a Twix."

"You can have a Twix."

## Chapter 4 — The Bench

Fred's was the same Fred's it had been in 1985. The same Korean family had run it the whole time — the second generation now, the children of the parents who had bought it when Ruth had first moved to the neighborhood. Mrs. Park was at the register. Mrs. Park nodded at Ruth. Mrs. Park always nodded.

They got a Snickers and a Twix and two cans of grape Crush. Ruth paid in cash. They went outside and sat on the bench in front of the store. The bench was the one Ruth and Edward used to sit on when they were dating in 1971. Ruth had sat on that bench for fifty-five years. The bench knew her.

She opened her Snickers. She handed Marcus the napkin from inside the bag.

"Marcus. The other thing I want to say."

"Yeah."

"You did the right thing telling me. That was the hardest part. The next things — me calling Mrs. Holzer, me learning the apps, you and me figuring out what to do — those are easier than the part you already did. The hardest part is the kid telling the adult. You did the hardest part. I am proud of you for the hardest part. I want you to know that."

Marcus did not say anything. Marcus was twelve. Marcus could not say *thank you Gramma* without crying, and he did not want to cry on the bench in front of Fred's where his friends might walk by.

He did the small thing twelve-year-old boys do when they want to say something but can't. He leaned his head against her shoulder for one second. One second. Then he straightened up and bit into the Twix.

Ruth ate her Snickers. She watched the leaves on Elmwood. She thought about Caroline overseas. She thought about Edward at the cemetery on Lawson Road. She thought about Marcus on the bedspread Edward had picked out in 1997. She thought about how the world Marcus was twelve in was not the world Caroline had been twelve in, and was not the world she had been twelve in, and was not the world Edward had ever known to be twelve in.

She thought: *I am the only adult in this house. The figuring-out is mine. I will figure it out.*

She drank some of her grape Crush.

"Marcus. Let's walk back. I want to start the figuring-out today. I need you to show me what an AI app looks like when you open it. We're going to sit at the kitchen table and you're going to teach me. *I* am going to take notes. You are going to be the teacher today. OK?"

"…OK Gramma."

"And on Monday I'm calling Mrs. Holzer. From the kitchen. You can be there or you can be at school. It is your call."

"…I want to be there."

"Then you'll be there. We'll do it before school. We'll go in late if we need to."

They walked back up Elmwood. The hill was steeper on the way home. Ruth was seventy-two. She walked slower than Marcus. Marcus walked slower than he could, on purpose, to stay next to her. Neither of them mentioned it. Both of them knew.

## Chapter 5 — Sunday Morning

Sunday morning. Six-thirty AM. Ruth was at the kitchen table with the laptop her son-in-law Aaron had set up for her three years ago and which she had used mostly for Solitaire and the church bulletin email. The laptop was open. The notebook from yesterday was next to it. The notebook had three pages of Marcus's careful printing in it from where he had taught her, on Saturday afternoon, what an AI app looked like when you opened it, what a *prompt* was, what an *image generation* tool did, why some of them had safeguards and why some of them didn't.

Ruth had asked Marcus, before bed, what he would name an AI if he had one of his own to talk to that wasn't on his school account. Marcus had thought about it for a long time. Marcus had said, finally, *Pop.* After Edward, who Marcus had called Pop until Marcus was nine and Edward had died.

Ruth had cried in the laundry room for two minutes after Marcus had gone to bed, with the door closed, so Marcus would not hear.

Now it was Sunday morning. She was going to install the thing Marcus had told her about. The free one. The one that lived on the laptop, not somewhere else. She was going to learn it the same way she had learned to use the email in 2008 and the Solitaire in 2013 and the church bulletin in 2017 — slowly, in a chair, with a notebook, with a kid at the kitchen table who would teach her the parts she got stuck on.

She was going to call Mrs. Holzer at seven-thirty AM tomorrow. Marcus was going to be in the chair next to her at the kitchen table.

She was going to keep being the only adult in this house and she was going to be a good one at it for as long as her body let her.

She wrote in the notebook, on a fresh page:

> *What I do not know yet about AI. (To learn with Marcus.)*

She wrote, underneath: *most of it.*

She underlined *most of it* three times.

Then she opened the browser and typed in the address Marcus had written down for her on Saturday afternoon. The page loaded. She clicked the button that said *Get Started.* She made herself a fresh cup of coffee while it downloaded. She sat back down. She waited.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a small kitchen in a house Ruth had lived in for forty-one years, between a seventy-two-year-old grandmother and a twelve-year-old boy whose parents were on the other side of the world, who would learn together, badly and then slightly less badly, every weekend for as long as Ruth's hands worked and her eyes could read and her heart could keep loving Marcus the way it loved him now.

Marcus came down at seven-fifteen. He stood in the doorway of the kitchen in his pajamas. He saw the laptop. He saw the notebook. He saw Ruth's coffee.

"Gramma. Are you doing it."

"I'm doing it. Sit down. I need help."

He sat down. She turned the laptop toward him. They started.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Ruth's hand on the bedspread Edward picked out in 1997, by Marcus's head on her shoulder for one second on the bench in front of Fred's, and by the *most of it* Ruth underlined three times in the fresh page of the notebook. They were:

- **Inquiry** — Ruth knocking on the door and saying *I want to ask you what's going on. You don't have to tell me. I want you to know I'm asking.* The asking that opens a door without forcing it through is the move.
- **Humility** — Ruth: *I am seventy-two years old. I do not understand AI. I have not used it. I do not know how the apps work. I am going to learn. I am telling you this so you know I know I do not know yet.* Said to a twelve-year-old by his grandmother on a sidewalk in October. Variable 2 in plain clothes, said by the oldest character in the series so far.
- **Inclusion** — *I am going to ask you to help me learn, because you know the apps better than I do, and you are twelve and I am seventy-two.* The grandkid as teacher of the grandmother is Inclusion at the most direct level. The Tale takes it literally on Sunday morning.
- **Responsibility** — *I am the only adult in this house. The figuring-out is mine. I will figure it out.* The Variable lives in the absence of any other adult to defer to. Ruth cannot pass it. Ruth doesn't try.
- **Perseverance** — Ruth is seventy-two. Learning the laptop the first time was hard. Learning the AI is going to be harder. She is going to do it anyway, every weekend, badly and then slightly less badly. *I am going to keep being the only adult in this house and I am going to be a good one at it for as long as my body let me.*

The AI-generated image entered Marcus's twelve-year-old life *on* him — through somebody else's app, on a kid at school's terms, using Marcus's face without his consent. The local AI Ruth is about to install on Sunday morning is going to enter both of their lives *with* them — Ruth at the keyboard with Marcus next to her, both learning, both naming what they don't know, both going to call Mrs. Holzer at seven-thirty AM Monday from the same kitchen table. Same family. Same week. Opposite consent geometry. The shape is choosable, even at seventy-two with a notebook and a fresh cup of coffee at six-thirty AM.

### If you want to know what other people are doing about AI-generated images of kids, and grandparents raising grandkids in a tech transition

A short, honest map:

- **The National Center for Missing and Exploited Children (NCMEC)** runs the **CyberTipline** for reporting AI-generated sexual or harassing imagery of minors. Calling them is free and is the first call to make in cases more serious than the one in this Tale.
- **Common Sense Media** publishes guides specifically for grandparents and other kinship caregivers raising children — the *I am not the parent and I am the only adult here* shape.
- **The AARP** has begun publishing AI-literacy materials for adults sixty and over that treat the older learner as a peer, not as a remedial case. Rare and refreshing.
- **Take It Down** (run by NCMEC, free, anonymous) is a service that helps minors get nonconsensual sexual images of themselves removed from major platforms. Does not solve the AI-generated case completely but is part of the toolkit.
- **For schools:** the **Cyberbullying Research Center** has a new framework for how schools should respond to AI-generated images of students by other students. As of 2025 this is still very much being written; Ruth's call to Mrs. Holzer is the kind of call that helps the framework get written.
- **For the Are-Self side:** the **[Getting Started](../getting-started)** page is the on-ramp Marcus will walk Ruth through. Free, MIT-licensed, runs on the laptop you already own.

These resources don't all agree with each other. They don't all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, sit at the kitchen table with the kid, and start.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.
