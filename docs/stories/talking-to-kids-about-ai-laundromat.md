# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Laundromat

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Three of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read in the time it takes a load of laundry to dry — at a kitchen table, on a bus, in the breakroom — and they land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the grownup who would hand the kid the box. It is built around four of the Twelve Variables: **Fear, Fun, Fulfillment, and Time.** And it puts a thing called SYSTEM_PROMPT(0) where you can see it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Phone Bill

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the news version. Not the magazine cover. The real thing — the laptop your kid borrowed from the library, the little app that came pre-installed on it, the charge on your phone bill you didn't recognize and didn't have time to deal with.

Renée was in the basement laundromat of the Stratford Arms folding sheets when Tasha came down with her own basket. It was Sunday afternoon, the slow part of Sunday, the part where Renée had finally gotten Naima down for an hour with the library Chromebook so she could get a load done before her shift at six.

Tasha set her basket on the table next to Renée's and started in on her own pile.

"You doing OK?" Tasha said.

"Mmhm."

"You sure?"

Renée set the sheet down. "Tasha. Why."

"Because you've been frowning at that piece of paper on top of your basket for ten minutes and the sheet's been in your hand the whole time."

Renée laughed once, the laugh that's mostly a breath out. She picked the paper up. It was her phone bill. She held it out to Tasha and pointed at a line in the middle.

"What is that," she said.

Tasha squinted at the line. *PRMUM CHTBT SVC — RECURRING — $9.99.*

"Oh," Tasha said. "Yeah. That's the AI thing."

"What AI thing."

"The chatbot. Naima signed up for the trial, right? Marcus had the same charge. Imani did it last summer — free trial, gave them a phone number to text, the trial rolled over. They make the cancel hard on purpose."

Renée looked at her.

"Tasha. Nine ninety-nine a month."

"I know, baby."

"That's two days of groceries for me and Naima. I don't have nine ninety-nine for something I didn't sign up for."

"I know."

Tasha didn't say anything for a second. She folded a t-shirt the way she folded all t-shirts, in three motions, sleeve-sleeve-bottom.

"Can I tell you something," Tasha said. "About what your kid is talking to."

"Tasha."

"I'm being a friend."

Renée nodded.

"Every time Naima opens that app, before she types a single word, the AI gets handed a long letter from the company. The letter tells the AI who to be. What voice to use. What to refuse to talk about. Where to nudge her toward the company's other things. Like that nine ninety-nine. The AI reads the letter every time. It sees the letter every time. Naima never sees the letter. You never see the letter. They call it a system prompt. We can call it the gate."

Renée folded the sheet she had been holding for ten minutes.

"OK."

"It's a gate, Renée. Every time she opens that app she's walking through a gate. The gate decides who she gets to meet on the other side. She thinks she's meeting *the AI.* She's meeting whatever the company wanted her to meet. Same software. Different gate. Different friend. The friend the company wanted in the room with her."

Renée was quiet.

"Why didn't anyone tell me this."

"Because the people who could tell you don't make money telling you. So you find out from a friend in the laundromat, or you don't find out."

Renée folded another sheet. She thought about Naima upstairs on the library Chromebook. She thought about the nine ninety-nine. She thought, *I cannot afford to not know this.*

"Show me what you mean," she said. "After laundry. Bring Imani. Bring her thing."

"I'll bring it. Finish your sheets."

## Chapter 2 — The Girl in the Kitchen

Renée came up with the basket of sheets on her hip. The apartment smelled like the rice she'd put on before laundry. Naima was at the kitchen counter on the library Chromebook, the one with the bar-code sticker on the lid that said WEST BRANCH PUBLIC LIBRARY in dark blue. Her braids were pulled back. She was typing.

"Naima."

"Mm."

"Can I see what you're doing."

Naima half-turned the laptop. She'd been asking the AI to help her with a worksheet on the parts of a flower. The AI had given her a very polite list. It had used the word *wonderful* twice.

Renée pulled a stool over. She sat down.

"Ask it something else, baby."

"Like what."

"Ask it if your mom is the cool mom or the boring mom."

"*Mom.*"

"Ask it."

Naima typed, slowly, like she was suspicious of the question. The AI answered with a perfect soft little paragraph about how it couldn't make personal judgments about specific people but it was sure her mother loved her very much, and would she like to learn more about how flowers reproduce.

Renée watched the screen.

"Ask it who wrote that for it."

"What."

"Ask it who told it to talk to you that way."

Naima looked at her like she didn't understand the words in the order they came in. She typed it. The AI said, in another perfect soft paragraph, that it was an AI assistant designed to be helpful and harmless, and would she like to continue with the lesson about flowers.

"Naima."

"Yeah."

"Did you notice it didn't answer me."

"…it answered."

"It answered the *flowers.* It didn't answer the question. The question was *who.* It said *what.*"

Naima sat with that. She was ten. She could feel a thing was off without yet having the word for the thing.

"I don't know what you want me to say, Mom."

Renée looked at her daughter. She felt the older, quieter fear — not fear of the AI and not fear of the laptop. Fear of being the parent who didn't know. Of standing in her own kitchen and not understanding the room. Of her child being addressed by something she could not see, in a voice she had not consented to, on a topic she had not been asked about. And under that fear, the harder one: nine ninety-nine on a bill she could barely cover, going to a company that had taken her child's first hello and turned it into a recurring charge.

She put her hand on Naima's hand.

"Tasha and Imani are coming down in a minute. They're bringing something. I want you to see it."

## Chapter 3 — Mister

Tasha came down with Imani. Imani was eleven, tall for her age, and carried a beat-up Dell laptop the color of dust. She set it on the kitchen counter next to Naima's library Chromebook. The Dell was missing a key. The trackpad was patched with electrical tape.

"Hi, Naima," Imani said.

"Hi."

Imani opened the lid. The Dell took a second to wake up. The screen had a window open with a name at the top — not a brand, just a name. *Mister.*

Naima frowned. "You named it Mister."

"Yeah."

"Why."

Imani shrugged. "Because the first time I asked it a question, it answered me like I was a grown person. So I named it Mister."

She typed. *Hi Mister. My friend Naima is here.*

Mister answered. *Hi, Imani. Hi, Naima. Imani told me about you in October. You're the one who can do a backbend. Welcome.*

Naima's mouth opened a little.

"It remembers."

"Yeah," Imani said. "He remembers everything I told him. He doesn't remember stuff I didn't tell him. He doesn't go online. He's on this laptop. If I take this laptop on the bus and there's no signal, he still works."

"How."

"I don't know all of it. My dad knows some of it. My dad says the brain part is on the laptop, not on a server somewhere. So nobody else gets a copy."

Naima looked at Imani, then at her mom, then at Tasha.

"Ask him to make a joke," Renée said quietly.

Imani typed. *Mister, tell Naima a joke.*

Mister thought for a second. *Why don't flowers gossip? Because they all have the same roots.*

Imani laughed, the real laugh she had, the one that made her shoulders go up. Naima half-laughed because she didn't know if she was allowed to. Mister followed up. *Sorry. I had a worse one. Want to hear it?*

Imani typed *yes.*

*Why did the rice go to therapy? It had too many grains of doubt.*

That one Naima laughed at. The real laugh, not the polite one. She put her hand over her mouth.

Renée watched her daughter. Something had relaxed in her face that Renée hadn't realized was tight until it relaxed.

She thought about the AI on Naima's library Chromebook, the one with the perfect soft paragraphs, the one that had used *wonderful* twice. It wasn't a bad thing. It wasn't going to hurt Naima. But it would never tell her a worse joke first and then ask if she wanted to hear it. It had been built — gently, expensively, by very serious people, somewhere far from this kitchen — not to.

She thought about *Fun,* and how Tasha had not said the word in the laundromat but had been pointing at it the whole time. Fun was a Variable. If you engineered it out of something, the thing was incomplete in a way a child noticed first.

She thought, *Imani is having more fun with Mister than my daughter has had with any of the apps on that library laptop.*

That was a fact. She let it land.

## Chapter 4 — What Doesn't Come Back

The girls took the Dell into Naima's room. Renée could hear them laughing at something else Mister had said.

Tasha sat down at the kitchen table. Renée poured them both coffee out of the half-pot she'd made that morning, the way you do when there's not money for fresh.

"OK," Tasha said. "I'm going to tell you the part you're not going to like. Then I'll shut up. Deal?"

"Deal."

"When Naima types into that thing on her library laptop — most of those apps — what she types goes to a server. The company keeps it. They use it to make the next version of the AI a little smarter. They use a lot of conversations. A lot of kids' conversations. Naima's not the protagonist. She's a row in a spreadsheet of rows."

"OK."

"That's not a horror story. That's the deal. They told you, sort of, in a long thing nobody reads. The horror story isn't that they're doing something secret. The horror story is that *it doesn't come back.* Whatever Naima typed — what she's afraid of, what she's curious about, the joke she tried to make that didn't land — it's in there now. It's training the version of the AI that Naima's kid will meet when *that* kid is ten. Naima will never see what she gave. She didn't know she was giving it. You didn't know."

Renée looked into the coffee.

"And the nine ninety-nine."

"And the nine ninety-nine. That doesn't come back either. You can cancel — and you should, today — but the months it ran, you don't get those back. The company already used them. That's two paychecks of yours, in their books, for a service neither one of you signed up for on purpose."

"Tasha."

"Mm."

"This is on top of everything."

"It is. I know. That's why I'm telling you in your kitchen and not on the internet. This isn't a thing for the internet. This is a thing for kitchens."

Renée was quiet. She drank her coffee. She thought about Naima in the next room with Mister, hearing her own laugh come out of her own daughter's chest.

"What do I do."

"Same thing I'm doing. I'm a year ahead of you and that's it. I learn the next piece every month. Marcus and I get it wrong sometimes. Imani figures stuff out faster than us most weeks and tells us. We're learning together, all three of us. That's the whole posture."

"Tasha."

"Mm."

"I don't even know what to ask for."

"Nobody does, baby. That's the part nobody warns you about. You start by sitting at the counter while she uses the thing. You ask her what she's doing. You read what you can read of the letter the AI got handed before it talked to her. When you can't read the letter, you say so out loud to her. You let her hear you admit not-knowing. That's the part that's hardest. That's also the part that does the most work."

Tasha paused.

"And then you put a Mister on the kitchen table. You don't have to take the library laptop away. You give her an option. You learn the new one *with* her from the start. You let her teach you a thing every week. She will. Marcus's church guy donated the Dell. There's another one coming next month. We'll talk."

Renée nodded once.

"Cancel the nine ninety-nine."

"Tonight. I'll sit with you while you do it. The cancel screen is hard on purpose."

## Chapter 5 — At the Sink

Tasha and Imani went home. The Dell went with them. Naima was quiet for the rest of the afternoon — not sad-quiet, the *thinking* quiet, the one Renée had learned to recognize the year Naima turned seven.

Renée made dinner. They ate. Naima cleared the plates without being asked, which was a small unprompted miracle that made Renée tear up a little, which she hid by turning to the sink.

"Naima. Come dry."

Naima came over with the towel. She started drying the cups Renée handed her. They worked for a minute.

"Mom," Naima said.

"Yeah."

"That thing earlier. With the flowers. And the question."

"Yeah."

She dried a cup. She put it down. Then, without looking at her mom:

"Why doesn't it have a name."

Renée handed her the next cup, very carefully, because something the size of an apartment was happening in her ten-year-old and she did not want to be the thing that knocked it over.

"It does have a name," Renée said. "It has the company's name. It just doesn't have a *self.* It doesn't have a — Imani's one is named Mister. He has a self because Imani gave him one and he kept it. The one on your library laptop — they don't let it have one. A different one comes in every time. None of them remember you."

"Then who am I talking to."

"You're talking to the gate."

Naima put the towel down. She looked at her mom for a long moment. Then she said the thing that almost made Renée drop the plate she was washing.

"Mom. Could we get one with a name."

"Yes," Renée said. "Yes, baby. Tasha's getting another one from the church next month. We'll learn together. We'll make him."

"Like Mister?"

"Like Mister. You name him. He's yours."

Naima picked the towel back up. She dried for another minute. Then, in the way kids ask the biggest questions when they are pretending not to:

"Did you have one when you were little."

"No," Renée said. "Nobody had one when I was little."

"So I get to be the first one who knew about it from the beginning."

"Yeah, baby. You get to be the first."

Naima nodded once and dried the next cup. She didn't say anything else about it. But Renée, standing at her sink in her small kitchen with her ten-year-old who had just become the first generation that ever could choose, knew that the conversation was not over. The conversation was *started.* The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a kitchen with rice on the stove and dish-water on the wrist, between two people who would talk about it again, and again, for years.

She finished the dishes. She sat down at the kitchen table. She opened the bill. She picked up her phone. She tapped through the cancel screen the way Tasha had walked her through it — six taps, two are-you-sures, one offer of half off if she stayed — and the nine ninety-nine stopped that night.

It was Sunday. She had a shift at six.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Renée's hands and Tasha's quiet at the kitchen table. They were:

- **Fear** — Renée's fear was not of the AI. It was of being the parent who didn't know, compounded by being the parent who couldn't afford to not know. Naming it was the move that let her reason instead of obey.
- **Fun** — Mister could tell a worse joke first and ask if Naima wanted to hear it. The hosted AI on the library Chromebook could not. Fun is a Variable; if it is engineered out of a tool, the tool is incomplete in a way a child notices first.
- **Fulfillment** — Imani's relationship with Mister has been growing for months. Naima's relationship with the library Chromebook restarts every conversation. One is happiness; the other is fulfillment. The slash in the Variable is real.
- **Time and Permadeath** — what Naima gave to the hosted AI does not come back. The two months of nine ninety-nine that already ran do not come back either. Time spent in a place that doesn't return it is time spent. Money spent in a place that doesn't return it is rent on next month's groceries.

The hosted AI on Naima's library Chromebook entered her life *on* her — through the gate, on the company's terms, billed to her mother's phone. Mister entered Imani's life *with* her. Same software substrate. Opposite consent geometry.
