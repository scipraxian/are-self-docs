# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Campfire

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Eight of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

This is the scoutmaster's Tale. The grownup whose troop is his kids in the way teachers' classes are theirs and parents' families are theirs — different shape of relationship, same weight.

It is built around five of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Responsibility, Perseverance, and Inclusion.** And it puts a thing called *the merit badge requirement* at the campfire, where it can be looked at honestly by two adult leaders who have been doing this long enough to know the easy answer is wrong.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Talk Owen Didn't Write

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the troop-meeting version. Not the parent-handout version. The real thing — the kid you have been mentoring for four years pulling out his phone in the back of the dining hall after lunch, scrolling through a five-minute Communication merit badge talk he did not write, in the wrong font, with the headers still bolded the way the chatbot bolded them.

Hank had been a scoutmaster for fourteen years. Before that he had been an airline mechanic for thirty-one. He had hands that knew the right knot before his brain did and a way of standing at flag that the older boys recognized without being told. He was sixty-three. He had outlasted the boys' mothers a few times — kids he'd had at eleven, who came back at nineteen as Eagle candidates, who he had to project-manage through their last service hour with the same patience he'd had when they were tying their first half-hitch.

He had caught Owen at one-fifteen on the Saturday of the spring campout. Owen was fourteen, working on Communication, third class four years ago and wearing the rank as if it was a coat that had finally fit. He had been in the back of the dining hall after lunch, scrolling.

"Owen."

"Hi Mr. Hank."

"Show me what you're scrolling."

Owen showed him. The talk was three pages, single-spaced. The headers were bolded. The font was the chatbot's default font. Owen's name was at the top in a slightly different font than the body, the way it goes when you copy-paste something into a fresh document and Word does that thing.

Hank read the first paragraph.

"Owen."

"Yeah."

"Did you write this."

Owen did the thing fourteen-year-old boys do when they have already decided to lie and then hear the question and decide, in the air between the question and the answer, not to lie. He did the small breath. He did the look-at-the-floor.

"No sir."

"OK. We'll talk tomorrow morning. Before flag. Walk with me to the latrine path. Ten minutes. Just you and me. OK?"

"OK Mr. Hank."

"Don't tell anybody. This isn't a punishment thing. It's a we-need-to-figure-it-out thing."

"OK."

Hank had walked away without raising his voice, without a frown, without any of the flag-codes the boys in the troop had learned to read off his face since they were eleven. He had walked back to the chuck box. He had begun chopping the onions for the chili the patrols would cook for dinner. He had finished the onions before he let himself notice he had not actually figured out what to say to Owen yet.

That was nine hours ago.

It was nine forty-two PM. The fire was at the second-cup-of-cocoa stage. The boys were in their tents. Lights-out had been called fifteen minutes ago. Dave Pham, his ASM and Owen's father, was on the log across from him with a mug of cocoa in both hands and the face of a man who knew Hank had something to say.

"Hank."

"Dave."

"You've been chewing on something since the dining hall."

"I have been."

"Owen?"

"Owen."

Dave nodded, slowly. He didn't ask what. He waited.

## Chapter 2 — What the Tool Is

"He had a chatbot write his Comm talk," Hank said.

"Ah."

"He admitted it. He's not in trouble. I don't know how to handle it. I want to talk it through with you because you know what these things are and I don't, and because he's your kid, and because if I get it wrong tomorrow morning I will have done it wrong with somebody who has been wearing the uniform on my front porch since he was a Bear."

"OK," Dave said. "Where do you want me to start."

"Start with what the thing is. I've been hearing about it for a year. I haven't sat down with it. I'm going to."

Dave took a long pull of his cocoa.

"OK. The plain version. There's a kind of computer program called a large language model. Big math machine. Trained on a lot of text. You type a question, it produces an answer that sounds like writing a person would do. It's not thinking the way you and I think. It's predicting what word probably comes next, with the math turned up so high that the predicting feels like thinking. Most of them are run by big companies on big servers. You type into your phone, the words go to the company, the company runs the math, the answer comes back. Some of them you can run on your own laptop and the words don't go anywhere. Same kind of program, different living arrangement."

"OK."

"For Owen, what he probably did is open the app on his phone, type *write me a five-minute Communication merit badge talk on listening,* and the chatbot wrote one. Took him forty seconds. Not zero work. He had to ask. But not the work the merit badge was asking for."

"What was the merit badge asking for."

"Comm requirement four. *Prepare and present a five-minute talk to your troop or patrol on a topic of your choice.* The work is the prepare and the present. Not the words on the page. The Scout having lived with the topic. The Scout having organized his own thinking. The Scout having said it out loud in front of his patrol with his own voice. The chatbot did the words on the page. It did not do the having-lived-with."

Hank was quiet. He looked at the fire.

"Dave. What does the troop policy say."

"The troop policy doesn't say anything. We haven't written one. The Council put out a memo in November that said *be aware that scouts may be using AI tools for merit badge work* and that was it. They didn't tell us what aware was supposed to look like. So we're aware. And here we are."

"And Owen is the first one we caught."

"Owen is the first one we caught who left the headers bolded. There are probably other ones we didn't catch."

## Chapter 3 — The Scout-Correct Move

Hank took a long pull of his own cocoa. The cocoa was the powdered kind. The cocoa had been the powdered kind on every campout since 1979 when somebody had decided the troop wasn't going to switch to the milk-based kind because it didn't carry well.

"Dave. I want to figure out what the *scout-correct* version of this looks like before I talk to him in the morning. Because the scout-correct version isn't *no AI ever.* I know that much."

"Why isn't it."

"Because we use tools. Scouts use tools. The whole program is *learn to use the right tool the right way for the right job.* Knife. Compass. Topographic map. We don't say *no compass, learn the stars.* We say *learn the stars and the compass and when to use which.* The chatbot is a tool. The question is how to use it the right way for the merit badge."

"OK Hank. So what's the right way."

Hank looked at the fire for a long minute.

"The merit badge is asking the boy to learn to give a talk. The talk is the work. The talk is the part the boy is supposed to grow inside of. The chatbot writing the talk for him gives him the words but skips the growing. So the chatbot can be a tool, but it can't be the doing. It can be — it can be the older scout he asks for help. He can ask the chatbot questions about listening. He can have the chatbot explain what active listening is. He can argue with the chatbot about whether listening is a skill or a habit. He can ask the chatbot to give him five examples of bad listening from history. All of that is the chatbot helping him *learn.* But when he sits down to write the talk, the talk is his. When he stands up to give the talk, the words are his. When the patrol asks him a question after, the answer is his."

"You just wrote the troop policy at a campfire."

"Did I."

"Yes Hank. You did. It's good. *Use the chatbot to learn. Don't use the chatbot to do.* That's a sentence we can say to the boys at the next troop meeting. That's a sentence Owen can take into Comm tomorrow morning."

"Dave. There is a thing I need to be honest about with him. I haven't sat down with the chatbot myself. I don't know what it feels like to use it. I'm going to ask him to walk me through it tomorrow. *He* shows *me.* That's part of what I want to do."

"Hank."

"Mm."

"That is the thing he needs to see. The scoutmaster he's been listening to since he was eleven saying *I don't know this thing yet, show me.* That moment is going to land harder than anything else you say to him tomorrow."

Hank nodded.

He had done the same thing with topographic maps, when topographic maps had become digital, in 2008. He had asked one of the older scouts at the time to show him how the GPS thing worked. The scout had been seventeen. The scout had been Eagle within the year. The scout had still talked to Hank twice a year on Hank's birthday and on the anniversary of Hank's wife's passing. *I don't know this thing yet, show me* had been the move that had made the relationship.

Same move. New tool.

## Chapter 4 — Before Flag

Six-fifty AM. Dew on the canvas. The boys were stirring in their tents but not out yet. Flag was at seven-fifteen. Hank was at the picnic table by the chuck box with two mugs of coffee. He had made the second one for Owen.

Owen came out of his tent at six-fifty-three. He saw Hank. He walked over. He sat down on the bench across from him. Hank slid the second coffee across.

"Owen."

"Mr. Hank."

"You want sugar in that?"

"…Two please."

Hank handed him the sugar packets. Owen opened them. He stirred. He did not look up.

"Owen. Did you write any of the talk."

"I wrote my name. At the top."

"OK. Tell me what you were trying to do when you opened the chatbot yesterday."

Owen thought about it. He blew on his coffee.

"I had to give the talk on Sunday. I hadn't started. I was scared I wouldn't have time. I asked the chatbot to write it because then I could just read it. I knew it wasn't the right thing. I did it anyway."

"Owen. Thank you for telling me the truth."

"…OK."

"Here is what I want to do. I want you to walk me through how the chatbot works. Right now. Before flag. You show me. *I* don't know how it works. I have not sat down with it. I want to learn it from you. Then we are going to figure out together what the *scout-correct* version of using it for Comm requirement four looks like. *Then* you are going to give the talk on Sunday — your own talk, your own words, the chatbot helped you learn but didn't write it — and I am going to sign off on the requirement. Deal?"

Owen looked up.

"Mr. Hank. You want me to teach *you* how to use the chatbot."

"Yes."

"…OK."

He pulled out his phone. He opened the app. He held the phone so Hank could see.

"OK. So you type in here. Like — what do you want me to ask it."

Hank thought.

"Ask it what *active listening* is. In two paragraphs. Plain language. Like it's talking to a fourteen-year-old."

Owen typed. The chatbot answered. Hank read the answer. The answer was actually pretty good. Hank would not have written it as well himself.

"Owen."

"Yeah."

"That's a useful tool. That tool just helped us understand active listening better than the merit badge book does. So that part — using it to learn — is good. We are going to use that for the troop. We are going to call that the *learn part.* Are you with me."

"Yes Mr. Hank."

"And the *do part.* The talk you give the patrol on Sunday. The standing up, the saying the words, the answering the questions after. That part the chatbot can't do. That part is yours. Always. *That* is what the merit badge is testing. That is what *Be Prepared* means here. Not preparing the words on the page. Preparing the *self that gives the talk.* The self is yours. The chatbot can't be your self. You have to grow it on your own."

"OK Mr. Hank."

"Owen. I am going to write a one-page thing for the next troop meeting. *Use the chatbot to learn. Don't use the chatbot to do.* I want you to help me write it. You and me at the picnic table after the meeting. Because you understand the chatbot better than I do and I understand the merit badge structure better than you do. We are going to write the troop policy together. You good with that?"

"…Yeah Mr. Hank. Yeah."

"OK. Drink your coffee. Flag in twenty."

---

## Chapter 5 — Flag

Seven-fifteen. The boys were in formation. The flag was at the base of the pole. The senior patrol leader called them to attention. Hank stood at the front. Dave was beside him. Owen was in his patrol's row, third from the left.

The flag went up. The boys saluted. The senior patrol leader called *at ease.*

Hank stepped forward.

"Scouts. One thing before patrol corners. We are going to start having a conversation in this troop about how we use the new chatbot tools — the ones some of you have on your phones. I am going to be honest with you. I have not used them myself. I am going to learn from one of you. The patrol policy on this is going to be written by me and one of you together. That conversation starts at the next troop meeting. If you have been using these tools for merit badge work, you are not in trouble. We are going to figure out together what the right way to use them is. The wrong way to use them is the way the boy doesn't grow. The right way to use them is the way the boy grows more. We will figure out where the line is, in this troop, together."

He let it sit.

"OK. Patrol corners. Breakfast in twenty minutes."

The boys broke up. Owen passed Hank on the way to his patrol corner. Owen did not look up at him. Owen did the small thing with his shoulders fourteen-year-old boys do when they want to say something but don't have the words. Hank squeezed his shoulder once on the way past. That was enough.

Dave came up next to him.

"Hank."

"Dave."

"You're going to outlive me, aren't you. You're going to outlive all of us."

"I plan to."

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, on a campout in the chaparral hills on a Sunday morning at seven-twenty AM, between a scoutmaster who had not yet sat down with the new tool and a fourteen-year-old who was about to teach him, and would teach him again, and would be back at twenty-five with an Eagle on the wall and a wife and a kid and the same scoutmaster asking the same first question.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Hank's chopping the onions, Dave's two-handed cocoa, and Owen's small-thing-with-his-shoulders at flag. They were:

- **Inquiry** — *Tell me what you were trying to do when you opened the chatbot yesterday.* The question Hank asked Owen at the picnic table. Inquiry that opens a door rather than closing one is the move.
- **Humility** — *I have not sat down with it. I want to learn it from you.* A scoutmaster of fourteen years asking a fourteen-year-old to teach him a tool is the Variable in plain clothes.
- **Responsibility** — Hank handled it the same night, before sleep. The leadership move was *don't let it slide.* The follow-through was *don't make it punishment.* Both are Responsibility.
- **Perseverance** — Hank has been doing this for fourteen years. He will be doing it for ten more. The troop policy will get refined every year. The ripple is years long.
- **Inclusion** — Hank wrote the next troop policy *with* Owen, not *about* Owen. Inclusion at the policy-drafting table, not just at the receiving end of the policy.

The chatbot Owen used entered his merit-badge work *on* him — through the algorithm, on the company's terms, with the headers still bolded. The conversation Hank had with him over coffee at six-fifty-three AM entered Owen's scouting *with* him. Same kid, same merit badge. Opposite consent geometry. The shape is choosable, even in a forty-minute window before flag.

### If you want to know what other people are doing in scout programs and youth organizations right now

A short, honest map:

- **Boy Scouts of America (Scouting America as of 2024)** has not yet published a national AI-use policy for merit-badge work as of this writing. Council-level guidance is patchy and inconsistent. Troop-level policy is being written by individual scoutmasters and committees in real time, the way Hank and Owen are writing it in this Tale.
- **4-H** has begun publishing AI literacy curriculum aimed at the 8–18 range, framed as *learn the tool, learn its limits, learn when to use it.*
- **Code.org** and **CSforAll** have free K–12 materials that extend naturally into youth-organization settings.
- **The merit-badge structure itself** — *learn it, do it, teach it, sign off* — is a remarkably good frame for AI-use policy without needing a national directive. *Use the tool to learn. Don't use the tool to do.* That sentence is the whole policy in one Tale.

These resources do not all agree with each other. They do not all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, hold the disagreements open, and write your troop's policy with your scouts in the room.
