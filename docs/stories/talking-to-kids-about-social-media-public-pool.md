# Have You Talked to Your Kids About Social Media? — A Public Pool

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Five of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read on a lounge chair while your kid is in the deep end with two of her friends, and they land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the grownup whose kid has had the app for a year and who hasn't, until tonight, scrolled the whole feed.

It is built around four of the Twelve Variables: **Permadeath, Perception, Time, and Fear.** And it puts a thing called *the algorithm* where you can see it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Lounge Chair

Have you talked to your kids about social media?

Not the news version. Not the morning-show segment. The real thing — the post your kid made when she was eight that is still up, the comment a stranger left on it that is still there, the algorithm that learned what your kid liked before she knew it herself.

Tessa was on a faded blue lounge chair at the Southside Public Pool with her phone face-down on her stomach and a paperback she had not opened since June. It was Saturday afternoon. Sage was in the deep end with two of her friends, doing a thing where they took turns pretending to be drowned and then rescuing each other, which had been the core gameplay loop since Sage was five.

Marisol came over from the other side of the pool with her flip-flops in her hand. Marisol was the school mom Tessa always ended up next to at curriculum night — they had been sitting next to each other once a year for four years and had achieved the comfortable acquaintanceship of women who don't quite know each other's last names.

"Tessa."

"Marisol. Hey."

"This seat free?"

"It's yours."

Marisol sat. She put her flip-flops underneath the chair the way she put her flip-flops under everything: precisely.

They watched the pool for a minute. Sage had just been "rescued" by her friend Maddie, who was now performing a CPR she had clearly choreographed in her own head.

"Your daughter is OK?" Marisol said.

"She's having the time of her life."

"No I mean OK. Like. OK."

Tessa looked at Marisol.

"What."

"I don't know how to ask this without it being weird so I'm going to ask it weird. Has she been on TikTok long?"

Tessa picked up her phone. She put it back down.

"About a year. We have an arrangement. She has it. I look at it sometimes."

"How sometimes."

"Like — once a month? When I think of it."

Marisol was quiet for a second.

"Tessa. My Andre. You know my Andre."

"I know your Andre. He's gotten so tall."

"He was eleven once. He was Sage's age once. He had TikTok at eleven the way Sage has it at ten."

"OK."

"When he was eleven, he made a video. He did a Spider-Man impression. It was bad. The impression was bad. But it was the kind of bad that other eleven-year-olds liked. The video got — I don't know, five thousand views. To Andre, five thousand views was the moon. He thought he was going to be famous. I was proud of him. I gave him a high-five. I bought him a sundae. I did not understand what five thousand strangers' eyeballs on my eleven-year-old meant. I was a parent, not a media person."

"Marisol."

"He's fifteen now. The video is still up. It is also up on three other accounts that I do not control because somebody reposted it. Kids at his new middle school found it last year. They quote it at him. Not in the friendly way, Tessa. Not in the friendly way. He came home twice in October and sat in his room. Twice. My Andre is the kid who used to sing in the car. He doesn't sing in the car anymore."

Tessa picked the phone up again. She did not open it.

"I'm sorry, Marisol."

"I'm not telling you to be sorry. I'm telling you what I wish somebody had told me. Has Sage made anything?"

"I don't know."

"Tessa."

"I haven't looked in three months."

"Look tonight. Look at the whole feed. The whole thing. From the first one. I'll watch your bag. Take the call later if you need to. I'll be here."

Tessa watched her daughter pretend to be rescued for what must have been the tenth time that afternoon. Sage was flopping, very specifically and on purpose, like the Disney *Little Mermaid* did when she got her tail caught in the rocks.

"OK," Tessa said. "OK."

## Chapter 2 — Forty-Seven

That night Sage was in the bath. Tessa sat at the kitchen table with her tea and Sage's iPad. She had Sage's password because they had an agreement.

She opened the app. She found Sage's profile.

There were forty-seven posts.

Tessa had thought there were maybe six.

She scrolled to the bottom. The first one was from January last year. Sage was eight at the time of the post. Tessa was eight at the time of the post too, in a sense, because Tessa had set up the account and watched the first three videos and then had not watched any of the others. The first video was Sage and her cousin Eli singing the Frozen song in the car. Tessa had been driving. She remembered the day. She did not remember Sage filming it.

The video had four hundred and twelve views. Sage's name on the account was *sageyM2017.* The account was public. It had always been public. Tessa had not known what *public* meant when she set it up, exactly. She had thought *public* meant *visible to family.*

She scrolled up. The posts told a year of her daughter she had not been watching. Sage and Maddie at the pool last summer. Sage doing a slime experiment in the kitchen. Sage doing a dance Tessa had seen Sage practice for a week. Sage with a kitten she had held at a friend's house — that one had nine hundred views, and a comment from a username Tessa did not recognize that said *prettyy girl.* The double *y.* The comment was from June. It had been on the post for ten months.

Tessa put the iPad on the table. She stood up. She walked to the sink. She drank a glass of water she had not poured. She walked back to the table. She sat down. She picked the iPad up again. She read the comment again.

She opened her own DMs to Marisol. She typed *can I call you tomorrow.* She did not send it yet. She left it in the box.

She thought about Marisol on the lounge chair. She thought about Andre in his room in October. She thought about Sage in the bath right now, ten years old, with forty-seven posts that were *public,* with a stranger's comment on her cat photo that she had probably never even seen.

She thought about the word *Permadeath.* She had never used the word in her life. She did not even know where she had heard it. But it was the word in her mouth right now. The post about the kitten was Permadeath. The comment on it was Permadeath. The four hundred and twelve views of an eight-year-old singing in the car were Permadeath. None of them came back. Sage, ten and in the bath, would be twenty and in college and the post would still be there.

She sent the message to Marisol. *Can I call you tomorrow.* Marisol replied in eight seconds. *Yes. Anytime. I love you.*

Tessa put the iPad down and put her face in her hands for a minute. Then she heard Sage open the bathroom door upstairs and she stood up and she made dinner.

## Chapter 3 — Sunday Morning

Sunday morning. Tessa called Marisol from the back porch with her coffee. Sage was still asleep.

"Marisol."

"Hi honey. You looked."

"I looked."

"How many."

"Forty-seven."

Marisol whistled, very softly.

"And there's a comment, Marisol. From a stranger. On a video of her with a kitten. It's been there for ten months. It just says *prettyy girl,* with two y's. Sage didn't tell me."

"Did you tell her you saw it."

"Not yet. We didn't have time after the bath. We're going to look together this afternoon."

"OK. Good. Look together. Don't make her watch you delete things in front of her. Don't make her think she did something wrong. She didn't do anything wrong. The platform did. The platform is doing what the platform was built to do. That's the part that's hard."

"Tell me the part that's hard. The whole part. I want it."

Marisol was quiet for a second. Then:

"OK. Here is the part that's hard. The platform makes its money by keeping eyeballs on it. That's the only thing it makes its money on. Everything else — the algorithm, the For You page, the notification at 9 PM, the stranger's heart on a kitten video — all of it is in service of keeping eyeballs on the app for one more minute. *Your kid's eyeballs.* Your kid is the product. The advertisers are the customer. The strangers are the audience. Sage is the *show.* The algorithm learned what makes Sage post again — what kind of comment, what kind of view count, what kind of tiny dopamine — and the algorithm gives her exactly enough of it to keep posting. The strangers? Some of them are kids. Some of them are not. The algorithm doesn't sort. It just serves."

"Marisol."

"And the things she posts don't come back. Andre's video is the loud version. The quiet version is every kid on every platform whose first awkward post at nine is up there at twenty-five when she's interviewing for her first job and HR types her name into a search bar. There is no shelf you can take it back from. *Permadeath.* My Andre's word, not mine. He used it about his Spider-Man video the first time he sat on the bed and cried about it. He had played a video game with the word in it. He knew what it meant. He looked at me and said *Mom, this is permadeath.* I had to look it up later."

Tessa's coffee was getting cold. She didn't drink it. She listened.

"Marisol. I don't even know what to ask for."

"Nobody does, honey. That's the part nobody warns you about. I'm four years ahead of you because of Andre, and that's it. I get it wrong every month. Andre teaches me a piece every couple of weeks. We're learning together still. You're going to do the same thing with Sage. You start by looking with her. You let her hear you say out loud the part you don't know. That's the part that does the most work."

"OK."

"And then — same posture as the looking — you start the small things. You ask her what she sees when she opens the app. You learn together that the heart counts and the view counts are *for the platform,* not for her — they're the platform's hooks, and you and she get to learn how to feel them coming and decide whether you want them. You both learn that the strangers on the app are not her friends. The friends in the pool are her friends. The strangers are the audience. She is allowed to have an audience. She is also allowed to not. And then — and this is the part nobody tells you — you make her a *real* place to be a kid online with her actual people. There are ways. There are friend-only apps. There are private chat groups. There are ways to make a thing that's just for Maddie and Eli and the cousins. The platform is one option. It is not the only option. She has been told, by the platform, that it is the only option. We can untell her — together, with her in the room when we do."

"OK."

"You OK."

"No. But I will be. I'll call you tonight after we look."

"Call me. I'll be home."

Tessa drank the coffee. It was cold. She drank it anyway.

## Chapter 4 — On the Couch

Sunday afternoon. Pretzel bowl on the couch between them. Tessa had told Sage in the kitchen, over snack, that she wanted to look at the app together — not to delete anything, just to look — and Sage, after a minute of tightness in her shoulders, had said *OK.*

Sage opened the app on her own iPad.

"Sage. From the bottom. The very first one."

"Mom. There are *forty-seven.*"

"I know. We've got time. Go to the bottom."

Sage scrolled. She scrolled past more posts than she remembered making. She got to the bottom. The Frozen video.

"Oh. I was *eight.*"

"You were eight."

They watched it. Sage was singing. The light was good. Eli was hitting all the harmonies.

"Mom. I forgot we did that."

"I forgot you filmed it."

"…I forgot too actually."

They worked their way up. Sage narrated some of them. *Oh that one's the slime one. Oh I forgot about this dance. Oh the kitten one — that's Maddie's kitten, that's not even my kitten.*

When they got to the kitten post, Tessa said:

"Sage. Look at the comments."

Sage scrolled the comments. She got to the one. *prettyy girl.*

"…Oh. That one."

"You saw it."

"I saw it. I just — I didn't know what to do."

"Sage. Tell me what happened in your head when you saw it."

"I didn't like it. I deleted the comment. But then they kept hearting my videos. So I stopped looking at the hearts so I wouldn't see their name. But I kept posting. Because the rest of the hearts were my friends and I wanted those."

Tessa nodded. She did not say anything for a second, because what Sage had just described was the entire shape of the trap, in Sage's own ten-year-old voice, perfectly.

"Sage. Honey. What you just told me — that is the *whole thing* the people who built the app want to happen. They build the heart count so you keep posting. They let strangers hit the heart so the count gets bigger. They make the heart from your friend look exactly the same as the heart from the stranger, so you can't tell at a glance which one is which. They do that on purpose. Because if you could tell at a glance, you would post less, and they would make less money."

"…oh."

"Sage. The hearts from your friends are real. The hearts from the strangers are the platform pretending to be your friends. They're not the same thing. You did the right thing deleting the comment. You also did a brave thing telling me. Telling me is the move that *keeps* the thing — I would never take away a thing that's making you safer to talk about. Take-away is for thing-that-hurts-you-and-you-won't-tell-me. This is the opposite."

"OK."

"Can we sit with it for a minute. Don't delete anything yet. Just sit."

They sat. Sage scrolled, slow. Tessa watched her daughter's face. Sage's shoulders had been tight at the start of the couch. They were not tight anymore. They were thinking-tight, which was a different kind of tight. Tessa could live with that one.

## Chapter 5 — At the Table

Sunday dinner. Tessa made the macaroni Sage liked. They ate. Sage talked about the pool day from yesterday and about a thing Maddie had said and about a video she was thinking of making about the rescue choreography but maybe she would just send it to Maddie instead of posting it.

Tessa noticed the *maybe.* She did not name the *maybe.* She let it sit.

When Sage had finished her macaroni, she put her fork down. Then, in the way kids ask the biggest questions when they are pretending not to:

"Mom. Could we make our own thing instead. Like — somewhere just for us. With Maddie and Eli."

Tessa did not cry into the dishes, because she was a parent and she had eaten enough macaroni at this table over the last ten years that she had a control on her face. She had a sixty-second window. She used twelve seconds.

"Yeah, baby. We can. I'll learn how. There are ways. Marisol said there are ways. I'll find out which ones are the right ones and we'll do it together."

"OK."

"And Sage."

"Yeah."

"You can keep posting on the other one too if you want. We can also just not post the next one. We can also delete the things you don't want anymore. *You* get to choose. You and me, together. The choosing is yours. The figuring-out-how-to-choose part is mine. OK?"

"OK."

She nodded once. She finished her macaroni. The dishwasher hummed under the counter the way the dishwasher always hummed. Outside the kitchen window the neighbor's lemon tree was in flower the way it was every May.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a small kitchen on a Sunday evening, between two people who would talk about this again, and again, for years.

Tessa cleared the plates. Sage helped. They did the dishes together. When the last dish was in the rack, Sage went to her room to text Maddie. Tessa stood at the sink for another minute.

She picked up her phone. She typed to Marisol: *we looked. she's OK. we're going to make a small thing just for the cousins. thank you.*

Marisol replied in four seconds. *I knew you would. give Sage a hug from me. I love you both.*

Tessa put the phone down. She finished her tea.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Tessa's hands on the iPad and Marisol's back-porch coffee call. They were:

- **Permadeath** — *None of them come back. Sage, ten and in the bath, would be twenty and in college and the post would still be there.* The post that's up at nine is up at twenty-five. The comment that lives on the kitten photo for ten months is live for the next ten years. The platform is built for the post; it is not built for the take-back.
- **Perception** — Sage's own description of the trap was the cleanest naming of it: *the rest of the hearts were my friends and I wanted those.* The platform builds the heart from the stranger to look identical to the heart from the friend, on purpose, because if a kid could tell at a glance, the kid would post less. Perception is engineered.
- **Time** — Tessa's *I haven't looked in three months* and Marisol's *I gave him a high-five and bought him a sundae* are the same Variable. The grownup's attention is also a finite resource the platform competes for. The platform wins by default if the parent never looks.
- **Fear** — Tessa's fear was not of TikTok. It was of being the parent who didn't know. Naming it as the parent-Tale fear lets her reason instead of obey. Marisol's playbook turned the fear into a weekend's worth of moves.

The platform on Sage's account entered her life *on* her — through the algorithm, on the company's terms, learning her dopamine before she knew the word. The friend-only thing Tessa is going to learn how to make for Sage and Maddie and Eli enters Sage's life *with* her. Same kid, same age. Opposite consent geometry.

If you want the technical companion to this Tale, the page on **[Context Windows](../context-windows)** explains why a tool that runs on hardware the user already owns has a different shape than a tool that runs on a server that needs the user's eyeballs. The page on **[Scipraxianism](../scipraxianism)** carries the full Twelve Variables and the Creed. *Mira and the Are-Self* — **[Book One of the Tales](./storybook)** — is the same philosophy spoken to the kid herself.

### If you want to know what other people are saying about all this

The conversation about kids and social media is loud and ongoing. A short, honest map of the rooms it's happening in:

- **Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024)** is the book most parents are quoting at each other right now. Its argument is that smartphone-and-social-media adoption between roughly 2010 and 2015 caused a measurable adolescent mental-health crisis, especially for girls. Worth reading, worth pushing back on, worth knowing.
- **Common Sense Media** publishes free, regularly-updated guides for parents on specific apps, age-rating recommendations, and family-media planning. Less ideological than Haidt, more practical.
- **The Center for Humane Technology** (Tristan Harris and colleagues) runs the *Your Undivided Attention* podcast and frames the platform-business-model critique that Marisol gave Tessa on the back porch in this Tale. *The Social Dilemma* (Netflix, 2020) is the documentary version.
- **Anya Kamenetz's *The Art of Screen Time*** is one of the most readable parent-facing books that does not lead with fear — closer in shape to the move this Tale tries to make.

These voices don't agree with each other. They don't all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, hold the disagreements open, and decide with your kid in the room.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.
