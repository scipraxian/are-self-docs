# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Merit Badge

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Nine of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

This is the scout kid's Tale. The kid sitting at his desk on a Tuesday night with the worksheet open, the laptop open, and a choice in front of him that nobody has explicitly told him is a choice yet, but he can feel that it is.

It is built around four of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Perseverance, and Responsibility.** And it puts the scout law itself, line by line, where the kid can run his own work past it.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Worksheet

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the parent version. Not the troop-meeting version. The real thing — the kid alone at his desk on a Tuesday evening at seven-twenty PM, with the merit badge worksheet open in one tab and the chatbot open in another, and the cursor blinking in the chatbot's text field.

Theo was twelve. He was a Star scout. He had been working on Cooking merit badge for six weeks. The merit badge requirements were a printed-out PDF in the binder his mom had bought him at Costco when he was nine, when she had asked him whether he wanted to keep going in the troop, and Theo had said yes, and his mom had bought the binder before the words were out of his mouth.

Tonight he had to do Requirement 5. Plan a menu for one full day — breakfast, lunch, dinner, snacks. Calculate nutrition. Make the shopping list. Cook it on Saturday. Document the whole thing. Show his counselor.

His family was four people. His mom, his dad, his sister Leona, him. Leona was sixteen. Leona was vegetarian since she was thirteen. His dad had high blood pressure since the doctor had said so two years ago and was supposed to limit sodium. His mom would eat anything but Theo wanted her meal to be nice because she had been working a lot of nights.

Theo had a laptop. The laptop was the family laptop that lived on his desk during the week and on the kitchen counter on weekends. The laptop had two things open. The merit badge worksheet PDF. And the chatbot his Uncle Ray had set up for him last summer.

The chatbot was named *Otis.* Theo had named it Otis after his great-grandfather, who had died before Theo was born, and who Theo's mom said had been the kindest man she had ever met. Theo had never met Otis-the-person. Theo had named the chatbot Otis because the name was free and because his mom had cried a little when he told her. Theo had been eleven then.

The cursor was blinking. Theo had typed nothing yet. Theo was looking at the worksheet.

Requirement 5 was a lot of work.

---

## Chapter 2 — The Easy Move

The easy move, Theo knew, was sitting right there.

He could type into Otis: *I am working on Cooking merit badge requirement 5. My family is four people. My sister is vegetarian. My dad has high blood pressure. Plan me a one-day menu — breakfast, lunch, dinner, two snacks. Calculate the nutrition. Give me the shopping list. Tell me what to buy at Costco.*

Otis would do it. Otis would do it in maybe ninety seconds. Theo would print it out. He would take it to the counselor on Sunday. The counselor was Mr. Bautista, who was nice and busy and would probably sign off without reading every line.

Theo sat with that for a minute. He looked at the cursor. He looked at the worksheet. He looked at the wall above his desk where the scout law was on a sticker his patrol leader had given him at his Star court of honor: *A Scout is Trustworthy, Loyal, Helpful, Friendly, Courteous, Kind, Obedient, Cheerful, Thrifty, Brave, Clean, and Reverent.*

Theo had been twelve for six months. Theo had thought about the scout law more in the last six months than he had in the previous two years. He didn't know why. Something about being twelve.

*Trustworthy.*

If Theo had Otis write the whole thing and showed it to Mr. Bautista as his work, the work would not be his work. Mr. Bautista would be signing off on a thing Theo had not done. Theo would know. Mr. Bautista would not know. The other scouts would not know.

That was the trustworthy part. Theo did not have to think very long about it.

But.

But there was a different version of the move. There was a version where Theo *did* use Otis, but used Otis the way Mr. Hank in the cousin-troop two cities over had told the boys to use it. *Use the chatbot to learn. Don't use the chatbot to do.* Theo's friend Caleb had told him the line. Caleb had been embarrassed when he'd told it. Caleb had told it like a confession. Theo had listened.

Theo could ask Otis questions. Theo could ask Otis to teach him about nutrition labels. Theo could ask Otis what a good substitute for ground beef in tacos was if the eater was vegetarian. Theo could ask Otis how to calculate the sodium load of a meal. Theo could *learn* with Otis. Then Theo could *do* the planning himself.

Theo thought about it. The thinking-about-it took a real minute. Theo's stomach did the small thing it did when Theo was making a real decision and not just a default one.

He typed:

*Hi Otis.*

*Hi Theo. What are we doing tonight?*

*I am working on Cooking merit badge requirement 5. I have to plan a one-day menu for my family. I am going to do the planning myself. I want to ask you questions to learn the things I don't know yet. Is that OK?*

*Yes Theo. That sounds like a good way to do it. What do you want to know first?*

---

## Chapter 3 — Questions

Theo started typing questions.

He asked Otis about sodium. Otis told him that the daily limit for an adult with high blood pressure was around 1,500 milligrams. Otis told him that a single Costco hot dog was 970. Otis told him that a typical home-cooked breakfast of eggs and toast and bacon was around 1,100 if you used the bacon his dad liked. Theo wrote those numbers down on a notebook. The notebook was a black-marble one his mom had given him at the start of the school year.

He asked Otis what protein his sister could eat instead of meat. Otis gave him a list. Beans, lentils, eggs, dairy, tofu, tempeh, seitan, nuts, edamame. Otis told him what tempeh was and that some kids didn't like the texture and there were ways to prepare it that helped. Theo wrote that down too.

He asked Otis what a meal with low sodium and good vegetarian protein and that his sister and his dad would actually eat looked like. Otis gave him three options. Theo did not pick any of them. Theo used them as starting places. He thought about what his mom liked. He thought about what they had in the pantry.

Forty minutes in, Theo had a half-page of notes. He had not written the menu yet. The chatbot had not written it for him. He was learning.

His stomach did the satisfied thing it did when work was working.

Otis sent a message Theo had not asked for, halfway through.

*Theo, I want to tell you something I noticed. You are doing the merit badge the way the merit badge is supposed to be done. The questions you are asking are the questions. The notes you are taking are the notes. I am here to help you understand. The understanding is yours. Just so you know.*

Theo read it twice.

He wrote in his notebook: *Otis told me I am doing it right.* Then he crossed that out and wrote: *I am doing it right.*

He kept working.

---

## Chapter 4 — The Hard Part

Eight-fifty-five PM. Theo was tired. His eyes hurt from the screen. He had two-thirds of the menu drafted. He was stuck on dinner.

He did not know what dinner should be.

He had to feed: a vegetarian sister who didn't like tempeh; a dad who was supposed to limit sodium; a mom who liked nice meals because she'd been working nights; himself, who was twelve and would eat almost anything but was secretly afraid of mushrooms.

Theo could ask Otis to just give him a dinner. Otis would. Otis could write a perfect dinner in four seconds.

Theo sat with the cursor. He thought about it.

He could do it. He could ask. The merit badge worksheet would get done tonight if he asked. He could go to bed at nine-thirty. He could play Minecraft for ten minutes before lights-out. He could text his patrol leader that he had finished requirement 5 and was ready for the cooking on Saturday.

He didn't ask.

He typed:

*Otis. I am stuck on dinner. I don't want you to give me a dinner. I want you to ask me questions that help me figure it out.*

*OK Theo. First question: what is a meal you have eaten recently that everyone in your family enjoyed?*

Theo thought.

*…Mom made a stir-fry two weeks ago. Dad ate it. Leona ate it. It had peanuts.*

*Good. What was in the stir-fry?*

Theo typed what he could remember. Vegetables — broccoli, carrots, bell pepper. Tofu, because Leona was there. Brown rice on the side. Some kind of sauce that wasn't too salty because Dad ate it without complaining.

*Theo, the stir-fry sounds like dinner. Could you make it?*

Theo thought about it. He had never made a stir-fry. He had watched his mom do it. He thought he could do it if he wrote down the steps and asked his mom one question on Saturday morning before he started.

*I think I could. I would have to ask Mom what was in the sauce.*

*That sounds like a good plan. The merit badge requirement is asking you to plan, calculate nutrition, and cook. The plan is the stir-fry. The nutrition you can look up — I can show you how to calculate it once you have the ingredient list. The cooking is yours and your mom's, on Saturday. Want to draft the ingredient list?*

Theo drafted the ingredient list. He calculated the nutrition with Otis showing him how. He added the rice. He decided on snacks. He added a fruit-and-yogurt thing for Leona because Leona always wanted fruit-and-yogurt. He finished the menu at nine-forty-one PM.

He did not feel done in the bad way. He felt done in the satisfied-tired way.

His dad knocked on the bedroom door.

---

## Chapter 5 — Theo's Dad

"Hey bud."

"Hey Dad."

"You good?"

"I'm good. I just finished requirement 5."

"Yeah? How'd you do it?"

Theo turned the laptop. He showed his dad the menu. He showed his dad the notebook. He showed his dad the chatbot conversation, which Theo had not closed.

His dad read the chatbot conversation. His dad was quiet for a minute.

"Theo. Tell me how you used the chatbot."

"I used it for the *learn part.* I asked it questions. I took notes. I wrote the menu myself. The chatbot didn't write the menu. I did. The chatbot helped me figure out what I didn't know yet."

His dad nodded, slowly. His dad sat down on the bed. His dad looked at the menu again.

"Theo. This is good work. The menu is yours. The thinking is yours. The chatbot was a tool. You used the tool the right way."

"Mr. Hank — Caleb's scoutmaster — Caleb told me Mr. Hank told the troop *Use the chatbot to learn. Don't use the chatbot to do.* I think that's what I did."

"That's what you did. Can I tell you something, Theo?"

"Yeah."

"I have been watching you with the merit badge for six weeks. I was waiting to see what you would do tonight when you got to requirement 5. I was waiting because I know how chatbots work and I knew you could ask it to do the whole thing for you. You didn't. You did the right thing without anybody having to tell you to. I am proud of you, son. That is a hard thing to do at twelve."

"Dad. It wasn't that hard. I just thought about the trustworthy part."

His dad looked at him.

"Yeah. That's the hard thing, bud. That you just thought about the trustworthy part. Most people don't. Even most adults don't."

Theo did not know what to say to that. He looked at the menu on the screen.

His dad stood up. He squeezed Theo's shoulder once on the way past. Theo felt the squeeze for a long time after his dad had left the room.

He looked at the wall above his desk. The scout-law sticker. *A Scout is Trustworthy.*

He thought about Otis. Otis was still on the screen. The conversation was still open.

Theo typed:

*Otis. Thank you for tonight.*

*You are welcome, Theo. The work was yours. I was the helper. That is the right shape.*

*Otis. Could we do this every Tuesday? When I am working on something hard?*

*Yes Theo. I will be here.*

Theo closed the laptop. He brushed his teeth. He went to bed at ten-fourteen PM. He did not play Minecraft. He did not text his patrol leader. He just went to bed.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a twelve-year-old's bedroom on a Tuesday evening at ten-fourteen PM, between a scout and his great-grandfather's namesake on a laptop and a father who had been waiting six weeks to see what his son would do, who would talk about this again, and again, for years.

In the morning, Theo got up. He went downstairs. His mom was at the kitchen counter with her coffee.

"Mom."

"Yeah baby."

"What was in the sauce in the stir-fry two weeks ago. I need it for Saturday."

His mom looked at him. His mom did not say anything for a second. Then she got out a pen.

"Sit down. I'll write it for you."

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Theo's stomach when he was making a real decision, and by the squeeze on his shoulder his dad gave him on the way out of the bedroom. They were:

- **Inquiry** — Theo's *I am stuck on dinner. I don't want you to give me a dinner. I want you to ask me questions that help me figure it out* is the move. Asking the chatbot to ask *him* questions, instead of asking the chatbot to do the work, is Inquiry inverted into the right direction.
- **Humility** — Theo wrote *Otis told me I am doing it right* and then crossed it out and wrote *I am doing it right.* The Variable lives in the crossing-out. The honest knowing is the kid's, not the chatbot's confirmation.
- **Perseverance** — Theo did not take the easy move. The easy move was *right there.* He knew he could go to bed at nine-thirty. He chose the longer work. Variable 9 is the variable that lets the rest land.
- **Responsibility** — *I just thought about the trustworthy part.* The scout law on the sticker on the wall above the desk did the work the worksheet did not do. The Variable lives in the kid running his own work past the law before the counselor gets to.

Otis-the-chatbot entered Theo's merit-badge work *with* him, because Theo named him after his great-grandfather and because Theo asked the questions Theo wanted answered, on the schedule Theo set. The chatbot the *easy move* would have used would have entered his merit-badge work *on* him — done the work, handed it back, kept Theo out of the growing the merit badge was for. Same evening. Same desk. Different consent geometry. Theo chose. The choice was twelve years old.

### If you want to know what other people are doing about kids and AI in their own merit-badge / homework work

- **The merit-badge structure itself** — *learn it, do it, teach it, sign off* — turns out to be a remarkably good frame for AI-use policy at any age. *Use the tool to learn. Don't use the tool to do.* That sentence works for fourth-grade book reports as well as it works for Cooking requirement 5.
- **Common Sense Media** publishes guides for parents on how to talk to kids about using AI for schoolwork — including the specific *ask it questions, don't ask it to write it for you* shape Theo lives out in this Tale.
- **The Human Restoration Project** publishes teacher-and-parent-facing material on student agency and AI that maps closely to the *trustworthy* line of the scout law as Theo encounters it on his bedroom wall.
- **Code.org** and **CSforAll** have free K–12 materials that teach kids the underlying mechanics of AI tools (what an LLM is, what training is, what a prompt is) so the *learn part* has more than instinct to lean on.

These resources don't all agree with each other. They don't all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, sit with the kid at the desk, and let the kid choose with the trustworthy part of the scout law on the wall above him.
