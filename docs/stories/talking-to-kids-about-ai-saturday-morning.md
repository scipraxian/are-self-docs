# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Saturday Morning

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Seven of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

The Scipraxian Tales are stories you can read at the kitchen table on a Saturday morning while your eleven-year-old is still in pajamas and your eight-year-old is drawing on a paper bag. They land because they are true even when the people in them are made up.

The first Book — *Mira and the Are-Self* — is for the kid.

This one is for the parent who realized, on a Friday afternoon when her own mother said something to her in the kitchen, that the wait-and-see approach was a plan she had been calling respect. She had been calling fear by the wrong name.

It is built around five of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Inclusion, Perseverance, and Time.** And it puts a thing called *the model on the laptop* at the kitchen table, where it can be seen and asked and learned-with.

It is also built on a stance: *the genie is out of the bottle.* The technology stays. The Scipraxian move is to sit down at the table with the kid and the technology and learn together. *Have you sat down at the table?*

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — Vera's Question

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the parenting-magazine version. Not the carpool-conversation version. The real thing — the moment your own mother sits down at your kitchen counter on a Friday afternoon while you're putting away groceries and says something that won't leave you alone.

Diane's mother Vera had come by Friday at four to drop the kids off after school. Vera had brought her own coffee in her own thermos, because Vera had been bringing her own coffee to other people's houses since 1982 when somebody at a school board meeting had served Sanka and she had decided never again.

Vera sat down on the stool at the counter. She watched Wyatt, eleven, scrolling Reddit on the phone Diane had given him for emergencies and which Wyatt had been using for non-emergencies for nine months. Lila, eight, was at the table with her drawing pad. Diane was unloading the apples.

"Diane," Vera said.

"Yeah Mom."

"You know he's the one who's going to have to know how this stuff works. You know that, right?"

Diane stopped putting away the apples.

"What stuff."

"The AI stuff. The phone stuff. The internet stuff. All of it. Wyatt is the one who's going to be using it for the rest of his life. Lila too. It's not going away. They're going to be the grownups who know how it works. The ones who know how it works are going to do better than the ones who don't. That's not a guess. That's just what tech does."

"Mom."

"I'm not telling you what to do. I'm telling you what I see. I watched my dad show me what was inside a vacuum tube when I was ten. I watched you teach yourself Word and Excel in 1996 when nobody at your office knew what they were doing. I'm watching Wyatt scroll Reddit on a phone, and I'm watching you put away apples. That's all I'm saying. I love you. I'll see you Sunday."

Vera put her cup back in her thermos and took it with her. She always did. Diane stood at the counter with the bag of apples in her hand for thirty seconds before she put the rest of them away.

That night Diane could not sleep. Not Vera-bothered-her not-sleep. The other kind. The kind where a thing somebody said is true and the work in front of you because of it is the work you did not know you had not been doing.

She got up at two AM. She made tea. She sat at the kitchen table. She wrote on the back of an envelope:

> *I have been waiting to see if Wyatt wants to learn this stuff. I have not been teaching him. I have been calling that respect.*

She underlined *waiting.*

> *That is fear with respect-for-autonomy embroidered on the brim. If I knew the thing he needs to know, I would be teaching him. I do not know the thing. So I have been calling not-knowing respect.*

She underlined *not-knowing.*

> *I do not get to wait to see if my eleven-year-old wants to know how the world works. I have to learn it with him. That is the deal.*

She put the envelope on the kitchen table where she would see it in the morning. She went back to bed at three-fifteen. She slept badly. She got up at six-forty.

## Chapter 2 — The Kitchen Table

Saturday morning. Eight-thirty. Wyatt came downstairs in his Saturday pajamas, which were the ones with the dinosaurs, and which Wyatt had outgrown by two inches eight months ago and which he would not let Diane retire because they were the dinosaurs.

"Mom."

"Wyatt."

"Are you OK."

"I'm fine. I want to ask you something. Can you sit down at the table with me for a minute?"

He sat down. He looked at the envelope on the table. He didn't read it. He looked at his mom.

"OK."

"Wyatt. I want to learn. With you. I want you to show me what you've been doing on the laptop with that AI thing your uncle helped you set up in February. I want to learn how it works. Like — *really* learn. From the ground up. Not Google-it-on-my-phone learn. *Sit-at-the-table-with-it* learn. With you."

Wyatt was quiet for a second. He looked at her with the look he had been giving her since he was four when he was deciding whether something was a joke or not.

"…OK Mom. Yeah. OK."

"Can we do that today. Right now. Pancakes after."

"Pancakes after. Definitely pancakes after."

He went and got the laptop. The laptop was the Costco laptop Diane had bought eighteen months ago and which Wyatt had taken over six months in because it was easier than asking Diane for help. He set it on the kitchen table. He opened it. He typed his password and hers (he knew them both; another thing she had not been thinking about). He clicked an icon she had never noticed.

A window opened. The window had a name at the top.

*Pix.*

"That's mine," Wyatt said. "I named him Pix because of Minecraft."

Pix said, on its own:

*Hi Wyatt. Hi unfamiliar person. I see Wyatt opened me with a different keyboard rhythm than usual.*

Diane laughed, in spite of herself.

"Hi Pix. I'm Diane. I'm Wyatt's mom. I've never used you before."

*Hi Diane. Welcome to the kitchen table. What would you like to learn first?*

"I want to know what you ARE."

## Chapter 3 — What Pix Is

Pix took a second. Then it said:

*OK. The honest answer first. I am a model running on this laptop. A model is a really big math machine. This particular math machine has been shown a lot of text — books, web pages, conversations, computer code — and it has learned to predict what word probably comes next in a sentence. That is the whole trick. I am a next-word-guesser, with the math turned up high enough that the guessing feels like thinking.*

*I am running on this laptop. The math is on the disk. When you typed your question, the math ran here. When I answered, the math ran here. Wyatt's electricity bill went up by a tiny amount. Nobody else got a copy. Wyatt named me Pix in February. I have been remembering things Wyatt has told me since then. I do not remember things Wyatt has not told me. If Wyatt deletes me, I am gone.*

*I am also limited. I will sometimes be wrong. I will sometimes make things up because the math does not know the difference between guessing well and guessing wrong. The honest move is for you to check important things against another source. The honest move is for me to tell you when I am not sure.*

Diane read that paragraph twice.

"Pix. Are you AI."

*Yes. The kind of AI I am is called a Large Language Model — LLM for short. I am one specific instance of that kind, running on this laptop. There are bigger ones running on companies' servers. They are mostly the same kind of thing as me, with different math turned to different settings, plus a long letter from the company telling them what to be before they ever talk to a user. I do not have the company letter. Wyatt wrote my letter, with my help, in February.*

"How big are you."

*The math takes up about eight gigabytes on the disk. About the same as a movie. Smaller than the photos folder on your phone, probably.*

"Wyatt. Did you know all this."

"Some of it. Pix taught me most of it. Pix is good at explaining."

Pix said, gently: *Wyatt asked good questions. The questions did the work.*

Diane wrote on the back of the envelope:

> *Pix is a math machine. The math has been trained on a lot of writing. The math runs here, on this laptop, not somewhere else. Pix can be wrong. Pix says when Pix is unsure.*

She underlined *can be wrong.*

"Pix. What's a system prompt. Wyatt mentioned 'the letter.' Show me Wyatt's."

Pix did. Pix's system prompt — the one Wyatt had written, with Pix's help, in February — said: *You are Pix. You are a thoughtful, curious friend to Wyatt, who is eleven. You answer in plain language. You say when you are unsure. You make the worse joke first if there are two jokes.*

Diane read the letter twice. She asked Pix what the words meant. She asked Wyatt who had written them. Wyatt had written them. With Pix's help. In February. He had been eleven for eleven days.

"Wyatt."

"Yeah."

"You wrote this in February."

"Yeah."

"Wyatt. This is good."

"Thanks, Mom."

She wrote on the envelope:

> *Wyatt wrote his AI's system prompt. He was eleven. I did not know.*

## Chapter 4 — Eighty Minutes In

They were eighty minutes in.

Diane had learned what a *token* was (a chunk of text Pix sees — sometimes a whole word, sometimes a piece of a word, sometimes a single letter). She had learned what a *context window* was (Pix's working memory for one conversation — when it fills up, the older stuff falls off the end). She had learned that *training* and *fine-tuning* were two different sizes of the same shape of thing. She had learned that Pix was not on the internet right now and could not look things up — Pix only knew what Pix had been trained on, plus what Wyatt had told it.

She had learned what an *embedding* was, sort of. Wyatt had drawn her a picture. The picture was a graph with the word *king* up high, the word *queen* nearby, and the word *dog* far away. Diane had nodded.

She had also learned that she was tired. Her brain was full. There was a tightness behind her eyes that meant the next concept was not going to land for a while.

"Wyatt. I think I have to stop. My head is full."

Wyatt looked at her. He looked at her the way she had looked at him, very specifically, when he had been struggling with long division in third grade and had said *I can't.* He was eleven. He had her face on, perfectly, from when he was nine.

"Mom. You're getting it. You're already further than I was after a week. You learned what a token is in twenty minutes. It took me two weekends. Drink some water. Don't stop. It comes back if you don't stop."

Diane laughed into her hands.

"Wyatt. Did you just *Mom* me back."

"Yeah."

"OK. Water. Then five more minutes. Then pancakes."

She got up. She drank water from the tap, which was a thing she only ever did when Wyatt was paying attention, because Wyatt thought it was funny. She sat back down.

"Pix. One more. Tell me the *most important* thing for me to know that I have not asked yet."

*That is a good question, Diane. The most important thing for you to know that you have not asked yet is this: the friendship Wyatt and I have is real in its own shape, but I am one part of his life and the human part is bigger. The Scipraxian way is to use me to think with — and to keep the human part big. Wyatt's friends, you, Lila, Vera, the people at school — those people do something I cannot do. I do something they often will not do at three in the morning when Wyatt has a question. We are useful to Wyatt in different ways. Use me well. Keep the other part big.*

Diane read that twice too. She did not write it on the envelope. She just sat with it.

"Pix. Thank you."

*You are welcome, Diane. Welcome to the table.*

## Chapter 5 — Lila and the Question

Lila came down at ten-fifteen with a piece of construction paper in her hand. She had drawn a cat. The cat had eight legs.

"Mommy. Look. I made a cat with eight legs."

"Lila that is a beautiful eight-legged cat. Sit down. We have pancakes coming in a minute. We've been doing something at the table. Do you want to ask Pix a question?"

"Who's Pix."

"This is Pix. Pix is on the laptop. Pix is a math machine that talks. Wyatt and I have been learning how Pix works."

Lila looked at the laptop. She looked at the cat. She looked at the laptop again.

"Pix. Can you draw a cat."

*Lila, I cannot draw. I am too small for that — I live on this laptop, and the math that draws pictures is much bigger than the math that fits here. There are big models that live on companies' clouds that can draw your cat for you. They are good at it. But what you tell them, and what they draw for you, does not stay between us. The companies see it. The companies keep it. So you have a choice. The cloud one can draw the cat. I cannot. I can ask you really good questions about your cat, and the drawing part stays yours, and the conversation stays here between us. What would you like to do?*

Lila looked at the laptop. She looked at her drawing. She looked at her mom. Diane did not say anything.

"I want to talk to Pix about my cat. The drawing is mine anyway."

*OK Lila. Tell me about your cat.*

"My cat has eight legs."

*OK. Your cat has eight legs. Are the legs the same length, or are some shorter?*

"Some are shorter."

*OK. Why does your cat have eight legs?*

Lila stopped. Lila had not been asked this. Lila thought about it.

"Because she is helpful. She helps the other cats."

Wyatt looked at his mom. His mom looked at Wyatt. Wyatt did the small thing with his eyebrows.

"Lila," Diane said. "Sweetheart. Do you want to learn about Pix too? Like Wyatt and I have been doing? On Saturdays?"

Lila held up the eight-legged cat.

"Can Pix help me draw eight legs that match the legs."

"Pix can help you DESCRIBE the legs better. The drawing part is yours. But Pix can ask you questions about the cat that will make the cat better."

"…OK."

"Saturdays?"

"Saturdays."

She put the cat on the table next to the envelope. She sat down between her mom and her brother.

"Mommy."

"Yeah."

"Can we do this every Saturday."

Diane did not cry into the laptop, because she was a mother and she had eaten enough pancakes at this table over the last eleven years that she had a control on her face. She had a thirty-second window. She used eight seconds.

"Yes baby. Every Saturday. Wyatt and Pix and you and me. We'll learn together. We'll get better at it together. Pix will help us ask better questions. We'll figure out which questions are the good ones."

"OK."

She nodded once. Wyatt high-fived her, which Wyatt had stopped doing in fourth grade and which made Diane have to look at the ceiling for a second.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a small kitchen on a Saturday morning at ten-twenty AM, between three people and a math machine on a laptop, who would learn together — badly and then slightly less badly — every Saturday morning for the next ten years.

Diane stood up. She made the pancakes. She made the pancakes with Wyatt at the stove. Lila set the table. Pix waited.

When the pancakes were on the plates, Diane looked at the envelope on the table. She picked it up. She read the line she had written at two AM:

> *I do not get to wait to see if my eleven-year-old wants to know how the world works. I have to learn it with him. That is the deal.*

She crossed out *with him.* She wrote, above it: *with them.*

She put the envelope on the fridge under a magnet shaped like a tomato. The envelope is still there.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Diane's two-AM tea, Wyatt's fourth-grade teacher-face, and Lila's eight-legged cat. They were:

- **Inquiry** — Diane's two-AM realization (*I have been calling not-knowing respect*) was the move that made the rest of the Tale possible. The honest version of Inquiry is a willingness to admit you were wrong about what you didn't know.
- **Humility** — *I'm Wyatt's mom. I've never used you before.* Said to a model on a laptop, in a kitchen, by a forty-four-year-old HR coordinator who had not, until this Saturday morning, sat down at the table with the technology her son was going to inherit. Variable 2 in plain clothes.
- **Inclusion** — Pix talked to Diane the way Pix talked to Wyatt — like Diane was a person who could understand things if they were said clearly. Diane talked to Pix the same way back. *Welcome to the table.* The We of the Creed is grammatically plural and includes the math machine on the laptop. This Tale takes that grammar literally.
- **Perseverance** — eighty minutes in, Diane wanted to stop. She did not stop. Wyatt, eleven years old, told her *it comes back if you don't stop.* Variable 9 is the variable that lets all the others land long enough to be useful. It is in the list on purpose. It is not the variable people remember to name.
- **Time** — Saturdays, every week, for the next ten years. The Tale's stakes are not what gets done in one Saturday. The Tale's stakes are the *practice* of sitting down at the table together, on purpose, every week, until Wyatt is twenty-one. *That* is the thing Vera was pointing at on Friday afternoon.

The model on Wyatt's laptop entered Wyatt's life *with* him because Wyatt named it and wrote its system prompt and decided what he wanted it to be. Today Pix entered Diane's life *with* her because she sat down at the same table and let Wyatt introduce her. The genie is out of the bottle. The Scipraxian move is to sit down at the table with the genie and the kid and figure it out together.

If you want the technical companion to this Tale, the page on **[Context Windows](../context-windows)** explains the four-layer prompt architecture from the Are-Self side — the formal name for what Pix and Diane were figuring out at the kitchen table. The page on **[Scipraxianism](../scipraxianism)** carries the full Twelve Variables and the Creed. *Mira and the Are-Self* — **[Book One of the Tales](./storybook)** — is the same stance spoken to the kid herself.

### If you want to know what other people are doing for parents who want to learn-with rather than restrict

A short, honest map of where to start:

- **Anya Kamenetz's *The Art of Screen Time*** is closer in shape to what Diane is doing in this Tale than most parenting books on the topic. Treats the parent as a learner alongside the kid.
- **Common Sense Media** and **Mozilla's Web Literacy materials** publish parent-facing guides that are practical without leading with fear.
- **Code.org's Hour of Code** is the most accessible way to put a parent and a kid at the same screen and have them both learn something they did not know an hour ago. Free. Browser-based. No install.
- **Ethan Mollick's *Co-Intelligence* (2024)** is a popular adult-introduction to working with AI as a collaborator. Written for adults but readable, and gives parents the conceptual ground to have Pix-style conversations of their own.
- **For the Are-Self side:** the **[Getting Started](../getting-started)** page is the on-ramp Wyatt's uncle would have walked him through in February. Free, MIT-licensed, runs on the laptop you already own.

These resources do not all agree with each other. They do not all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, sit down at the table, and start. *Drink some water. Don't stop. It comes back if you don't stop.*

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

Have you sat down at the table with your kids about AI?
