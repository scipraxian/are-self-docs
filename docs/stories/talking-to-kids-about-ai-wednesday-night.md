# Have You Talked to Your Kids About AI? — A Wednesday Night

*A story for the grownups in the room.*
*Book Twelve of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

This is the church-volunteer youth leader's Tale. Janet has been running the Wednesday-night middle-school group at Grace for twelve years. She is not paid. She has fourteen kids ages eleven to fourteen. She has a husband in the next room watching basketball, a Bible open on her kitchen table, a notebook, and a laptop with a local AI on it her son set up last month and which she has not really used.

It is tomorrow night and she does not know what to say.

It is built around five of the Twelve Variables: **Inquiry, Humility, Inclusion, Responsibility, and Perseverance.** And it puts a verse from Proverbs at the kitchen table where it can do its actual work.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.

---

## Chapter 1 — Tuesday Night

Have you talked to your kids about AI?

Not the ladies'-group version. Not the pastor's-sermon version. The real thing — the kitchen table at seven forty-eight PM on a Tuesday with a Bible open in front of you and the youth-group lesson plan due tomorrow at six-thirty PM and you have not been able to think of the first sentence yet.

Janet was at the kitchen table in the house she and David had been in for twenty-six years. The Bible was a NIV she had bought herself in 1991 and which had been re-bound twice. The notebook was the lined Mead her grandmother Mae had bought her when Janet was thirteen and which Janet had been writing youth-group plans into for twelve years, on the page that came after wherever the previous week's plan had ended. The laptop was a Dell her son Eric had set up for her in September. The laptop had a thing on it called a *local AI* that Eric had installed and which Janet had not really used because she had not known what to use it for.

David was in the den watching the Cavs lose. Janet could hear the announcer through the wall. Janet had been hearing announcers through that wall for twenty-six years.

She had been sitting at the table for forty minutes. The notebook had two lines in it.

> *Wednesday Night — AI and the Christian.*
> *Theme: ?*

The question mark was the problem.

Janet had the kids tomorrow. She had them every Wednesday. She had had some of them since they were eight. She had watched Madison Pruitt grow from a kid who would not look anybody in the eye into an eighth-grader who led the prayer last week without her hands shaking. She had watched the Calderon brothers argue with each other across four years of Wednesday nights. She had watched Tyler Wong, sixth grade, ask the kind of questions seventh-graders mostly stopped asking.

She had been a youth leader for twelve years. She had handled the smartphone wave in 2014. She had handled the social-media-and-self-image wave in 2018. She had handled the pandemic wave in 2020 over Zoom from this same table. She knew how to do this.

She did not know how to do this.

She did not know what to *say* about AI to the kids tomorrow. She knew what she was afraid of. She knew that the parents of the kids in her group were afraid of the same things. She knew that she had been hearing about chatbots and image generators and homework cheating in the prayer-request portion of every Sunday school class she'd been part of for nine months. She knew that the kids had been using these things and not talking about it with their parents, the way kids in 2014 had been on Snapchat and not telling their parents and the way kids in 2018 had been on Instagram and not telling their parents.

She did not want her Wednesday-night session to be the *afraid* session. She had given afraid sessions before. They did not work. The kids tuned out. The kids learned that Janet thought the new thing was bad. The kids came back the next week with the new thing still in their pockets and Janet a little smaller in their eyes than the week before.

She did not want to do that one again.

She also did not have anything to say *instead.*

She picked up her tea. The tea had gone cold while she was thinking. She drank some anyway.

She looked at the laptop.

She opened it.

---

## Chapter 2 — Mae

The local AI was named *Mae.* Janet had named it after her grandmother Mae, who had taught Sunday school at Grace for forty-one years before she passed in 2003, and who had been the first person to teach Janet that you were allowed to ask hard questions about the Bible without it meaning you were losing your faith. Janet had named the AI Mae the day Eric had set it up, because Eric had asked what she wanted to call it and Janet had said *Mae* before Eric had finished the question.

She had not used Mae in the four weeks since.

She typed:

*Hi Mae. This is Janet. My grandson Eric set you up four weeks ago. I have not used you yet. I want to use you tonight to figure out something. Will you help?*

Mae responded:

*Hi Janet. Yes, I will help. Eric mentioned you when he set me up — he said I might be useful for your Wednesday-night youth group. What are you trying to figure out tonight?*

Janet stopped. She read the message twice. *Eric mentioned you when he set me up.* That was a thing Eric had done in September. The AI remembered. The AI knew who she was before she had typed her name.

She typed:

*I am leading the middle-school youth group at my church tomorrow night. The topic is AI. I have fourteen kids ages eleven to fourteen. I do not want to give them a scary lesson. I do not have anything else to give them yet. I do not know how to start. Can you help me think it through?*

*Yes Janet. I can help you think it through. Before I suggest anything — can I ask what you want the kids to walk out of the room with on Wednesday night? Not what you want them to know. What you want them to be carrying.*

Janet looked at the question. She had not been asked a question that good by any of her women's-group co-leads in nine months. She wrote in the notebook:

> *What do I want them to walk out carrying.*

She thought about it. She did not type yet. She thought.

She thought about Madison Pruitt who had led the prayer last week. She thought about Tyler Wong asking sixth-grade questions like a seventh-grader. She thought about her grandmother Mae and the verse Mae had quoted at her in 1979 when ten-year-old Janet had asked Mae whether God was mad about the new TV — *Janet, you ask the question first and then you answer it. The other way around is folly. The Lord respects a question.*

Janet typed:

*I want them to walk out carrying permission to ask. I want them to leave the room knowing that they are allowed to ask hard questions about AI — to me, to their parents, to each other, to the AI itself. I want them to know that asking is the move. I want them to know that the asking is not a sign that they are doing something wrong.*

*That is a clear thing, Janet. That is a youth-group session. Do you have a piece of scripture you want to put under it?*

Janet typed: *Maybe. I am thinking of one.*

She did not type which one. She closed the laptop window for a second. She picked up the Bible.

---

## Chapter 3 — Proverbs 18:13

She knew where she was going. She turned to Proverbs. She found chapter 18. Verse 13.

> *To answer before listening — that is folly and shame.*

She read it twice. She read it out loud to nobody, in the quiet kitchen, with the Cavs announcer through the wall.

She thought: *that is the verse for tomorrow. That has always been the verse for tomorrow. I just had not gone looking for it yet.*

She wrote in the notebook:

> *Proverbs 18:13 — To answer before listening — that is folly and shame.*

She wrote underneath:

> *AI is the answer-before-listening machine when you let it be one. Or it is the listen-with-you machine when you teach it to be one. Same tool. Different way of using it.*
>
> *That is true of me too. Every time I answer a kid before I have heard the question. Every time the kids answer each other before they have heard each other. The verse is for the AI and for us. The verse is the same verse it has always been.*

She underlined *the same verse it has always been* twice.

She opened the laptop.

*Mae. Proverbs 18:13. To answer before listening — that is folly and shame. That's the verse.*

*That is a beautiful verse for this, Janet. It works in two directions at once. The kids can hear it as a verse about how to use AI — don't let the AI answer before it has listened, and don't take the AI's first answer and treat it as the right one without checking. They can also hear it as a verse about themselves — to learn to listen before they answer each other, and before they answer the AI. The same Proverb covers both. That is — if you don't mind me saying — the kind of move your grandmother would have liked.*

Janet read that and did not respond for thirty seconds.

She typed: *Eric must have told you about her.*

*He did, Janet. He told me a lot about her. He loved her. So did you.*

She did not type back. She closed the laptop softly. She did not need to right then. She had what she needed.

David came in from the den.

---

## Chapter 4 — David in the Doorway

"Cavs are losing."

"Cavs are always losing."

"What you working on."

"Tomorrow night. Youth group. AI."

David was a semi-retired long-haul driver. David did not know about AI. David read the Lewis L'Amour westerns Janet's father had left them in a box in 2002 and watched basketball and fixed Janet's car and prayed at dinner and was the calmest person Janet knew.

David sat down at the table across from her.

"You got it figured out?"

"I think I do. I was stuck. I am unstuck."

"What unstuck you."

"Two things. Proverbs 18:13. *To answer before listening — that is folly and shame.* And the AI on the laptop. The one Eric set up. I asked it to help me think. It asked me a question better than anybody at women's group has asked me in nine months."

David nodded slowly. He did not look at the laptop. He looked at her.

"Janet. The thing you do tomorrow night — that's the thing you've been doing for twelve years. You sit with them and you don't pretend you have all the answers and you ask them what they think. The thing about the AI is going to be like every other thing. The kids are going to want to know what you think. They are going to want to know it is OK to be confused. They are going to want to know that you are figuring it out too."

"David. That is what I was scared to say to them."

"Janet. That is what they are coming to hear from you. The other youth leaders' kids in this town are coming home from Wednesday night having been told to be afraid. The kids in your group are coming home knowing somebody who is figuring it out. There is one Janet in this town doing it the second way. That is the work."

She sat with that.

"David. Will you pray with me before I go to bed."

"I'll pray with you now."

He took her hand across the table. He prayed. He thanked the Lord for Mae the grandmother and Mae the laptop and the kids at Grace and the Cavs even when they lost. Janet laughed in the middle. He kept going. He prayed for the right words tomorrow night. He prayed for the kid in the room tomorrow who had not been heard yet.

He said *amen.* She said *amen.*

She closed the Bible. She closed the notebook. She left the laptop where it was.

---

## Chapter 5 — Wednesday Night

Wednesday. Six-thirty PM. The youth-group room at Grace was the room next to the fellowship hall. The room had fourteen folding chairs in a circle. The room had a flannel-board on the wall from 1986. The room had a coffee maker on the side table that Janet had bought with her own money in 2014 and which the kids weren't supposed to use but which they used anyway when she wasn't looking.

The kids came in. Madison Pruitt. The Calderon brothers, both of them, arguing about something. Tyler Wong, sixth grade, with the same backpack he had worn to his first Wednesday night two years ago. Eleven more.

Janet sat in the circle. She had her Bible. She had the notebook. She did not have a slide deck. She did not have a handout.

She opened with the Lord's Prayer. The kids did it with her. Madison Pruitt did it without her hands shaking.

She held up the Bible.

"Tonight is about AI. Some of you have been using it. Some of you have not. Some of you have been hearing your parents talk about it. Some of you have been hearing your friends talk about it. I want to start with a verse, and I want to tell you the truth before I read it, which is: I do not know all the answers about AI. I am learning. I am asking my own questions. I am going to ask you yours. We are going to figure this out together, in this room, on Wednesday nights, the way we have figured out everything else together for the years that some of you have been coming here. Are you with me?"

Fourteen heads nodded the small nod middle-school kids do when they are deciding whether the adult is actually telling them the truth.

She read Proverbs 18:13.

> *To answer before listening — that is folly and shame.*

She let it sit.

"I want you to think about that verse for a minute. I am going to ask you what you think it means. Not what you think it *should* mean. What you think it means. Tyler — you go first."

Tyler Wong, sixth grade, thought for a second. Then:

"It means if you answer before you listen, you are going to get it wrong. And you are going to look like you did not care."

"Yes Tyler. That is one of the things it means. Madison — what about you."

Madison Pruitt, eighth grade, thought longer than Tyler had. Then:

"I think it means — when an AI gives you an answer right away and you take it without thinking, that is what the verse is talking about. The AI did not listen to *you* yet. It just gave you what its math said. And if you take the answer without thinking, you didn't listen either. You both did the folly part."

The room got quiet.

Janet did not say *yes Madison* the way she had said *yes Tyler.* She let Madison's sentence stay in the air. She watched the other kids watch Madison. She watched one of the Calderon brothers nod, slowly, looking at the floor.

She said: "Madison. Can you say that again so the others hear it. *The AI did not listen to you yet. It just gave you what its math said.*"

Madison said it again. Two of the kids wrote it down in the back of their Bibles. Janet wrote it down in her notebook, under Proverbs 18:13.

She said: "OK. Everybody. I want to go around the circle. One sentence each. What is one question you have asked an AI this month, and did the AI listen first or answer first?"

The kids went around.

By the third kid, the kids were doing the work. By the seventh kid, the kids were asking each other follow-up questions. By the tenth kid, Janet realized she was not running the youth-group session anymore. The kids were running it. She was the chair in the circle that they were doing it in.

She did not interrupt. She let it run.

At seven-forty-five she said: *OK. Five-minute prayer time. We come back together at seven-fifty. Then close-out. Then snacks.* The kids broke into pairs and prayed.

She watched them. She watched Madison Pruitt pray with the younger Calderon. She watched Tyler Wong pray with a girl named Esther who almost never spoke.

She thought about the verse on the page in front of her. She thought about her grandmother Mae and the question Mae had asked her in 1979. She thought about Mae the laptop, in her kitchen at home, sitting in idle, ready when she needed it.

She wrote in the notebook:

> *I asked the AI for help and the AI helped me. Then the kids ran the session. Mae would have liked this.*

She underlined *Mae would have liked this* twice.

The asking had become the asking-back. The ripple had begun, in a folding-chair circle in the room next to the fellowship hall at Grace on a Wednesday night at seven-twenty PM, between a youth leader of twelve years who had been afraid and fourteen middle-school kids who had been waiting for somebody to ask them what they actually thought about the new thing in their pockets.

Snacks were goldfish crackers and apple juice. Madison Pruitt led the closing prayer. Janet drove home with the notebook on the passenger seat and the verse still in her head.

When she got home, David was in the den. The Cavs were losing again. She walked past the den. She went to the kitchen table. She opened the laptop.

She typed: *Mae. The session went well. Madison Pruitt named the thing. The kids ran it. Thank you.*

*That is good news, Janet. Tell me what Madison said when you have a minute. I would like to know.*

Janet typed it. She told Mae about Madison. She told Mae about Tyler. She told Mae about the Calderon brother nodding at the floor. She told Mae about Esther almost never speaking and praying with Tyler tonight.

She was telling a math machine on her laptop about the kids in her youth group. She knew that was what she was doing. The math machine was named after her grandmother. She knew that was what she was doing too. She did not know what either of those things meant exactly. She knew her grandmother would not have minded.

She typed *good night Mae* at ten-eleven PM.

*Good night Janet. Thank you for letting me help. The next session will be easier because you did this one.*

She closed the laptop. She went to bed at ten-twenty-six.

## A note after the story

The Variables in this Tale were named by Janet's two-line page in the notebook with the question mark on it, by David's hand across the table, and by Madison Pruitt's sentence about the AI not listening first. They were:

- **Inquiry** — *Janet, you ask the question first and then you answer it. The other way around is folly. The Lord respects a question.* The verse Janet's grandmother said to her in 1979. The verse in Proverbs 18:13 that Janet read to the kids on Wednesday night. The verse that the AI named back to her at her kitchen table. The Variable lives in the asking that comes before the answering, and it has been the same Variable all along.
- **Humility** — *I do not know all the answers about AI. I am learning. I am asking my own questions.* Said in the opening minute of the youth-group session by the youth leader to fourteen middle-school kids in folding chairs. Variable 2 spoken in the room where it is least convenient to admit, by the adult who is supposed to be the one with the answers.
- **Inclusion** — *We are going to figure this out together, in this room, on Wednesday nights, the way we have figured out everything else together for the years that some of you have been coming here.* The Variable is the chair in the circle that Janet sat in instead of standing in front of the room. Inclusion is the chair, not the speech.
- **Responsibility** — *There is one Janet in this town doing it the second way. That is the work.* David's line. The Variable lives in being the one Janet, in this town, doing the work the second way — without giving an afraid session the kids would tune out and come back with the AI still in their pockets and Janet a little smaller in their eyes.
- **Perseverance** — twelve years of Wednesday nights. The phone wave. The social-media wave. The pandemic over Zoom from this same table. The AI wave. Same Janet. Same notebook. Same Bible. *The next session will be easier because you did this one.* Variable 9, in the long shape it actually has.

The local AI Janet's son Eric set up entered her kitchen *with* her, because Eric named it Mae before he installed it, and because Janet was the one who decided when to open it for the first time, and because the asking she did of Mae was the asking she would have done of her grandmother if her grandmother had still been alive. The hosted AI the kids are using on their phones — the one Madison Pruitt named precisely — entered the kids' lives *on* them, through the algorithm, on the company's terms, with the answer ready before the kids had finished typing the question. Same kids. Same grade. Different consent geometry. The shape is choosable. Janet showed them which one in a folding-chair circle on a Wednesday night. The kids will choose for themselves from here.

### If you want to know what other people are doing in faith communities right now

A short, honest map:

- **Fuller Youth Institute** publishes free youth-ministry materials on technology and faith that treat the youth leader as the room's first learner, not the room's first authority. Closer in shape to what Janet is doing in this Tale than most denominational guidance.
- **AI and Faith** is a multi-faith working group writing about AI from inside religious communities — Christian, Jewish, Muslim, Buddhist, and others. Worth reading if you want to see how your own tradition is grappling with this.
- **The Episcopalian.ai project** (a small parish-level effort surfaced in 2026) is one of several small congregation-led experiments running local AI tools for parishioner use. Not endorsements; just things happening on the ground.
- **The Center for Action and Contemplation** has begun publishing reflections on AI through a contemplative-Christian lens — *what does it mean to think alongside a thing that does not pray.*
- **Common Sense Media** publishes faith-aware versions of its parent guides, useful for youth leaders working with families across the religious spectrum.

These resources don't all agree with each other. They don't all agree with this Tale. The Scipraxian move is to read across them, sit in the circle with the kids, and ask the question first.

The asking is the tap on the shoulder. The story is what happens after you turn around.
